POSH Child-Safety Position

Child-Safety Checks Should Be Free

If society expects adults to be checked before working with, supervising, coaching, mentoring, transporting, babysitting, or regularly being around children, then safety screening should be easy to access — not blocked by cost.

POSH believes child safety should be strengthened before harm happens.
That means easier checks, clearer written boundaries, stronger safe-adult accountability, and serious lawful consequences when trust around children is deliberately breached.

The POSH position

Child-safety screening should be treated as prevention infrastructure. The safer system is not fewer checks. The safer system is more accessible checks, better education, clearer boundaries, and stronger accountability.

POSH supports Working With Children Checks, police checks, child-safe standards, safeguarding policies, reporting pathways, and official screening systems.

But POSH also believes these systems should be easier to access. A person should not be discouraged from being checked because of cost, confusion, paperwork, or lack of awareness.

Child-safety checks should be free or government-funded wherever possible, because every extra checked adult strengthens the safety net around children.

Why cost becomes a safety barrier

Any fee attached to child-safety screening creates friction. Some people will delay it. Some will avoid it. Some will only get checked when an employer, club, school, or organisation forces them to.

Good adults delay checks

Safe adults may put off getting checked because they are short on money, unsure where to apply, or do not realise the check could help show transparency.

Families lose clarity

Parents are left guessing who has been checked, who understands child-safety boundaries, and who is willing to be transparent around children.

Prevention gets weaker

A system that waits until risk is already obvious is weaker than a system that makes safety screening normal, easy, affordable, and expected.

More checks, not fewer checks

The POSH approach is not to replace official checks. It is to stack safety layers together so families, clubs, schools, groups, and communities have clearer expectations around adults and children.

Safe Adult Accountability Agreement

POSH supports the development of a Safe Adult Accountability Agreement as an added child-safety layer. It would not replace official checks. It would record clear boundaries, adult responsibility, and prior knowledge.

What a safe adult should be willing to say

I will not create secrecy with children. I will not hide private contact. I will not pressure, groom, threaten, gift, isolate, sexualise, blackmail, or manipulate a child.

What the agreement records

The adult understood the boundaries, accepted responsibility, and acknowledged that child safety includes transparency, lawful reporting, and safe conduct online and offline.

What it helps prevent

It helps challenge secrecy, hidden private communication, unsafe digital contact, inappropriate gifts, emotional dependency, manipulation, and breach of trust.

What it supports later

If a serious breach occurs, the agreement may help show prior knowledge, accepted responsibility, breach of trust, and deliberate disregard for child-safety boundaries.

What this agreement is not

POSH must handle this carefully. Child safety matters, but fairness, lawfulness, privacy, and responsible use matter too.

Not proof someone is safe

A signed agreement does not guarantee safety. It is one layer of accountability, not a magic shield.

Not proof someone is unsafe

Refusing to sign cannot be treated as proof of guilt, wrongdoing, or danger. It simply means the person has not joined that accountability layer.

Not a public accusation system

This must never become a public naming, shaming, rumour, retaliation, or vigilante tool.

Not a replacement for authorities

It does not replace police, child protection, courts, safeguarding bodies, mandatory reporting, or official checks.

Why this also helps falsely accused safe adults

False allegations can seriously damage a person’s name, family, work, reputation, and mental wellbeing. A stronger safety system should protect children while also encouraging transparent, documented, fair adult conduct.

A safe adult cannot prove they are safe forever. No person can. But they can show a pattern of transparency, cooperation, lawful checks, written boundaries, child-safety awareness, and willingness to be held accountable.

That matters. It helps separate responsible adults who are willing to be visible from adults who rely on secrecy, confusion, hidden access, or weak boundaries.

The goal is not blind trust. The goal is documented trust, visible conduct, and safer systems around children.

The POSH proposal

POSH supports a stronger national conversation around child-safety screening, safe-adult accountability, free access to checks, and consequences for adults who knowingly breach child-safety trust.

1. Make child-safety checks free

Remove financial barriers so more adults can be checked, especially parents, volunteers, community helpers, carers, mentors, coaches, babysitters, and family support networks.

2. Normalise safe-adult declarations

Make it normal for trusted adults to acknowledge child-safety boundaries around secrecy, private contact, digital communication, gifts, pressure, and reporting responsibility.

3. Strengthen safeguarding education

Adults should understand grooming patterns, online contact risks, hidden apps, private messages, off-platform movement, child disclosure, and calm reporting pathways.

4. Treat deliberate breaches seriously

Where an adult knowingly breaches written child-safety boundaries, POSH supports stronger lawful consequences, especially where trust, authority, access, or secrecy was misused.

Stronger consequences for deliberate breaches

A private agreement cannot force a court outcome. But a signed child-safety agreement can still matter because it may show the adult knew the boundary before they crossed it.

Prior knowledge

The adult was told the child-safety boundary clearly before being trusted around children.

Accepted responsibility

The adult acknowledged the conduct that is not acceptable around children.

Breach of trust

The adult may have misused trust, access, authority, family connection, community role, or digital access.

Aggravating conduct

Where lawful and appropriate, a deliberate breach of acknowledged child-safety standards should be treated seriously.

Careful wording matters: POSH does not claim a private agreement can double sentences or replace a legal process. POSH supports the strongest lawful consequences available where adults knowingly breach child-safety trust.

What safe adults can do now

A safer community starts when adults stop treating child safety as someone else’s job.

Get checked

Apply for the official checks required in your state, role, workplace, club, school, organisation, or volunteer setting.

Be transparent

Avoid private secrecy with children. Keep communication visible, appropriate, and known to parents or responsible adults.

Learn the patterns

Understand grooming signs, emotional pressure, gifts, hidden chats, off-platform movement, and why children may stay quiet.

How this fits POSH

POSH is built around awareness, action, prevention, and safer systems. This page extends that mission from online safety into the wider adult accountability layer around children.

Children are safer when adults know the rules, parents know what to ask, organisations know what to document, and communities stop relying on silence or assumptions.

More visible adults. More accessible checks. More written boundaries. More prevention. Stronger lawful accountability.

Important disclaimer

This page is an educational POSH position and safeguarding proposal. It is not legal advice, not a criminal-history service, not a public register, not a background-check provider, and not proof that any person is safe or unsafe.

Families, organisations, clubs, schools, carers, and adults should continue using official Working With Children Check systems, police checks where appropriate, mandatory reporting pathways, child protection services, safeguarding policies, and legal advice when needed.

If a child is in immediate danger, contact emergency services or the appropriate child-safety authority in your area.
