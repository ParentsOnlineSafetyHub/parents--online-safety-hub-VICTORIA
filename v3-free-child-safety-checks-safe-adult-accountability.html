POSH Child-Safety Proposal

Free Child-Safety Checks & Safe Adult Accountability

POSH is proposing a voluntary safe-adult accountability layer that helps families, carers, sitters, teachers, coaches, clubs, organisations, and community networks create clearer child-safety expectations before trust is given.

Important: this is not a public offender register, not a public accusation system, not legal advice, not a background check, and not a replacement for Working With Children Checks, police checks, child protection, safeguarding policies, or official reporting pathways.

How The System Works QR / Member ID Agreement Layer Concern Pathway Build Roadmap

The simple idea

Official checks matter. POSH does not want to replace them. POSH wants more safety layers, not fewer.

The POSH Safe Adult Accountability Network would allow adults to voluntarily show that they accept clear child-safety boundaries, transparency, accountability, and proper reporting responsibilities.

A safe adult should not be afraid of clear child-safety expectations.

Why this matters

Parents need clarity

Parents are often expected to trust adults, clubs, programs, family networks, coaches, sitters, online contacts, and community spaces without a simple way to see who accepts child-safety accountability.

Safe adults need protection too

False or unclear accusations can destroy reputations. A transparent accountability system can help safe adults show that they understand boundaries and are willing to be accountable.

Children need safer systems

Child safety should not rely on gut feeling alone. It should be supported by culture, written boundaries, clear expectations, reporting pathways, and accountability.

POSH Safe Adult Accountability Network

The proposed system would not publicly label people. It would confirm whether an adult currently holds active POSH accountability status after they choose to share their POSH Member ID or QR code.

The public-facing result should be simple: status only.

POSH should confirm agreement status, not expose private history, rumours, reports, allegations, or sensitive personal information.

What POSH status would mean

It means the adult agreed to clear boundaries

They accepted written rules around secrecy, private contact, digital communication, gifts, manipulation, grooming behaviour, unsafe access, and reporting responsibilities.

It means there is a record of accountability

If concerns arise later, there is a signed agreement showing that the adult knew the expectations before being trusted around children.

It does not prove the person is safe forever

No system can prove that. POSH status would be one extra layer of visibility, prevention, transparency, and accountability.

It does not mean non-members are unsafe

A person who has not joined POSH is not automatically unsafe. They simply do not hold active POSH accountability status.

Member ID and QR verification concept

POSH should not allow people to search random names. A safer model is consent-based verification: the adult chooses to share their POSH Member ID or QR code with a parent, carer, organisation, club, school, coach, sitter, or safe-adult network.

Adult-controlled sharing

The adult in question provides their unique POSH Member ID or QR code. This prevents people from searching anyone’s name without consent.

QR code verification

A parent, carer, club, organisation, or family network can scan the QR code to confirm whether the adult currently holds active POSH Safe Adult Accountability status.

Member number search

If a QR code is not available, the adult can provide their unique POSH Member ID. The search should only work with that ID, not by public name search.

Status-only result

The verification result should only show limited status information, such as Active, Expired, Suspended Pending Review, or No Active Status.

Best privacy model: POSH should verify the agreement status, not expose private information.

The system should confirm that the adult has signed and currently holds an active Safe Adult Accountability Agreement. It should not operate as a name-search database, public register, accusation list, or character guarantee.

The agreement layer

The agreement is the core of the system. It creates a written record that the adult understood the child-safety expectations before being trusted in a child-related environment.

No secrecy with children

The adult agrees not to ask, pressure, reward, threaten, manipulate, or encourage a child to keep secrets from parents, carers, guardians, teachers, police, child protection, or safe adults.

No hidden private contact

The adult agrees not to privately message, contact, gift, pressure, groom, sexualise, isolate, threaten, blackmail, or move a child to hidden platforms without appropriate visibility and safeguards.

No grooming behaviour

The adult acknowledges that gifts, special attention, emotional dependency, secrecy, deleted messages, isolation, pressure, off-platform movement, and sexualised communication may be warning signs.

Reporting responsibility

The adult agrees to raise concerns through the appropriate parent, carer, guardian, safeguarding lead, child protection service, police, or relevant authority when a child may be unsafe.

Legal note: POSH should avoid promising “double sentences” or private punishment. The stronger lawful position is prior knowledge, signed responsibility, breach of trust, evidence preservation, and referral to proper authorities.

Free checks should be the standard

Child-safety screening should not be blocked by cost. If a community expects adults to prove they are safe around children, then access to basic safety checks should not depend on whether someone can afford the fee.

No financial barrier

The long-term position is that core child-safety checks should be free or government-funded so responsible adults can be checked without cost becoming a barrier.

More layers, not fewer

POSH status should sit beside official checks, not above them. A safer system uses multiple safeguards working together.

Accountability culture

Checks are not just paperwork. They should support a culture where safe adults expect transparency and unsafe behaviour becomes harder to hide.

Concern reporting pathway

If a concern is raised, POSH must not act like a court. POSH should preserve information, protect children, avoid defamation, and direct serious matters to proper authorities.

Concern received
↓
Immediate child-safety risk check
↓
Evidence preservation guidance
↓
Referral to authority where required
↓
Private temporary suspension if needed
↓
Remain active, suspend, expire, or remove

Core fairness rule: a report does not automatically mean guilt.

POSH must separate child-safety urgency from public accusation. Children must be protected, but adults must not be publicly labelled without lawful process.

What removal would mean

Removal from the POSH system must be handled carefully.

Removed does not publicly mean “unsafe”, “guilty”, “offender”, or “criminal.”

Removed only means the person no longer holds active POSH accountability status.

Serious concerns, evidence, and signed agreements should be directed to the appropriate authorities where lawful and necessary.

What this system must not become

Not a public offender register

POSH must not publish, recreate, expose, or attempt to replace any sex offender register.

Not a public accusation list

POSH must not become a place where people are publicly named, accused, attacked, shamed, or targeted.

Not proof of guilt

A report, refusal, expiry, suspension, or removal must not be treated publicly as proof that someone has committed an offence.

Not a name-search database

POSH should not allow people to search random names. Verification should be based on an adult choosing to share their QR code or Member ID.

Not a replacement for authorities

Police, child protection, courts, WWCC authorities, regulators, schools, and safeguarding bodies remain the proper pathways for serious concerns.

Not a guarantee of safety

Active POSH status should never be marketed as proof that an adult is safe forever. It is an agreement-based accountability layer.

Who could use this?

Parents & carers

To ask an adult to share their POSH QR code or Member ID before babysitting, sleepovers, transport, coaching, mentoring, or regular child contact.

Babysitters & family friends

To show they are willing to operate with transparency, boundaries, visible communication, and accountability.

Teachers & coaches

To support safer child-facing roles with clear expectations around communication, secrecy, grooming patterns, reporting, and conduct.

Clubs & organisations

To add a visible accountability layer beside existing safeguarding policies and official screening.

Safe adults

To show they take child safety seriously and are willing to be transparent about expectations.

Community networks

To help families build a stronger safety culture where adults understand the same rules and children know who they can talk to.

Build roadmap before the application

Before building the app, POSH needs the rules, documents, review process, privacy model, and safety wording locked down.

Step 1 — Public concept page

Publish this page to explain the system, invite feedback, and make clear what POSH is and is not trying to build.

Step 2 — Agreement PDF

Create a clean Safe Adult Accountability Agreement with identity fields, declarations, child-safety boundaries, signature, witness, renewal, and review wording.

Step 3 — Review pack

Prepare a legal, safeguarding, parent, organisation, and community feedback pack before allowing public sign-ups.

Step 4 — Privacy and data rules

Define what is stored, who can verify status, what appears publicly, how removals work, how disputes work, and when authorities are notified.

Step 5 — Pilot program

Test the concept with a small trusted group of parents, safe adults, coaches, carers, sitters, and safeguarding reviewers.

Step 6 — Application build

Build the first version with application forms, identity verification, agreement signing, QR codes, Member IDs, status checking, concern reporting, and admin review.

Future application modules

Adult application

Identity details, declarations, agreement acceptance, digital signature, optional WWCC/police check reference, and renewal reminders.

QR / Member ID verification

Adults can share a unique POSH Member ID or QR code so parents, carers, clubs, organisations, or safe-adult networks can verify active agreement status without searching by name.

Concern reporting

Structured reports, evidence preservation guidance, urgent-risk routing, private review, and authority referral prompts.

Admin review

Private case notes, document uploads, status changes, expiry, suspension, removal, and audit trail.

Organisation portal

Clubs, schools, groups, coaches, and community programs could manage verified adult participation.

Parent dashboard

Parents could save trusted adults, review child-safety boundaries, and access reporting and conversation tools.

Suggested status language

The wording matters. POSH should use neutral status language that verifies agreement status only. It should never publicly label someone as safe, unsafe, guilty, accused, or dangerous.

Active

This person currently holds active POSH Safe Adult Accountability status.

Expired

This person previously held POSH status, but it has expired and has not been renewed.

Suspended pending review

This status is temporarily unavailable while POSH reviews information under its safeguarding process.

No active status

POSH cannot confirm an active accountability status for this Member ID or QR code.

POSH should avoid public labels such as unsafe, dangerous, offender, banned, accused, predator, guilty, or flagged.

Review needed before launch

This system has potential, but it must be built carefully. The next step is review, not rushing straight into public sign-ups.

Legal review

Privacy, defamation, discrimination, data storage, consent, reporting obligations, dispute handling, identity verification, and wording.

Safeguarding review

Child-safe standards, disclosure safety, mandatory reporting, grooming indicators, organisational use, escalation rules, and trauma-informed response.

Technical review

Secure database design, authentication, audit trails, admin permissions, encryption, identity checks, abuse prevention, QR verification, and report handling.

Community review

Parents, carers, safe adults, coaches, teachers, babysitters, and organisations should be able to test whether the wording is clear and fair.

Official POSH position

POSH supports more child-safety checks, free or government-funded access to core screening, stronger adult accountability, clearer safeguarding culture, and better reporting pathways.

POSH does not support public vigilantism, public accusation lists, exposing private registers, or replacing proper legal and safeguarding systems.

The goal is simple: make safe-adult accountability easier to show, make boundaries clearer, and make unsafe behaviour harder to hide.

Help shape this before the app is built
