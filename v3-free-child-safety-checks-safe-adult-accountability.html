POSH Child-Safety Proposal

Free Child-Safety Checks & Safe Adult Accountability

POSH is proposing a voluntary safe-adult accountability layer that helps families, carers, sitters, teachers, coaches, clubs, organisations, and community networks create clearer child-safety expectations before trust is given.

Important: this is not a public offender register, not a public accusation system, not legal advice, not a background check, and not a replacement for Working With Children Checks, police checks, child protection, safeguarding policies, or official reporting pathways.

Proposed signup model: one-time $5 contribution only. The purpose is system maintenance, secure private records, administration, privacy protection, and safeguarding review — not profit, not access control, and not a financial barrier to child safety.

The simple idea

Official checks matter. POSH does not want to replace them. POSH wants more safety layers, not fewer.

The POSH Safe Adult Accountability Network would allow adults to voluntarily show that they accept clear child-safety boundaries, transparency, truthful reporting, accountability, and proper safeguarding responsibilities.

A safe adult should not be afraid of clear child-safety expectations — and a safe system should not be easy for manipulators, liars, or malicious reporters to weaponise.

Why this matters

Parents need clarity

Parents are often expected to trust adults, clubs, programs, family networks, coaches, sitters, online contacts, and community spaces without a simple way to see who accepts child-safety accountability.

Safe adults need protection too

False or unclear accusations can destroy reputations. A transparent accountability system can help safe adults show that they understand boundaries and are willing to be accountable.

Children need safer systems

Child safety should not rely on gut feeling alone. It should be supported by culture, written boundaries, clear expectations, reporting pathways, evidence handling, and accountability.

What POSH status would mean

It means the adult agreed to clear boundaries

They accepted written rules around secrecy, private contact, digital communication, gifts, manipulation, grooming behaviour, unsafe access, truthful information, and reporting responsibilities.

It means there is a record of accountability

If concerns arise later, there is a signed agreement showing that the adult knew the expectations before being trusted around children.

It does not prove the person is safe forever

No system can prove that. POSH status would be one extra layer of visibility, prevention, transparency, and accountability.

It does not mean non-members are unsafe

A person who has not joined POSH is not automatically unsafe. They simply do not hold active POSH accountability status.

One-time $5 contribution model

POSH should be built with the least possible barrier while still respecting the real cost of secure systems, private data, administration, records, and review processes.

One time only

The proposed signup contribution is $5 once only. It is not a subscription, not a recurring membership fee, and not designed to profit from child safety.

Used for secure maintenance

The purpose is to help maintain secure records, private data handling, storage, identity records, system security, administration, and safeguarding review capacity.

Refund on confirmed breach removal

If a member is removed after a confirmed breach of the agreement, the one-time $5 contribution should be refunded where lawful and practical. This helps show POSH is not profiting from removal, discipline, reports, or conflict.

Refund does not erase records

A refund does not erase the signed agreement, audit trail, concern records, breach finding, or lawful safeguarding obligations. Records may still be preserved and provided to proper authorities where lawful and appropriate.

Important: the $5 contribution should never be described as proof of safety. It is a small system-maintenance contribution only.

The agreement layer

The agreement is the core of the system. It creates a written record that the adult understood the child-safety expectations before being trusted in a child-related environment.

No secrecy with children

The adult agrees not to ask, pressure, reward, threaten, manipulate, or encourage a child to keep secrets from parents, carers, guardians, teachers, police, child protection, or safe adults.

No hidden private contact

The adult agrees not to privately message, contact, gift, pressure, groom, sexualise, isolate, threaten, blackmail, or move a child to hidden platforms without appropriate visibility and safeguards.

No grooming behaviour

The adult acknowledges that gifts, special attention, emotional dependency, secrecy, deleted messages, isolation, pressure, off-platform movement, and sexualised communication may be warning signs.

Reporting responsibility

The adult agrees to raise genuine concerns through the appropriate parent, carer, guardian, safeguarding lead, child protection service, police, or relevant authority when a child may be unsafe.

Truthful information

The adult agrees not to knowingly provide false, misleading, malicious, reckless, exaggerated, incomplete, or manipulated information through any POSH process.

No misuse of POSH

The adult agrees not to use POSH for personal disputes, revenge, harassment, reputation damage, manipulation, family conflict, workplace conflict, social conflict, or any purpose unrelated to genuine child safety.

Legal note: POSH should avoid promising private punishment. The stronger lawful position is prior knowledge, signed responsibility, breach of trust, evidence preservation, removal from POSH status where appropriate, and referral to proper authorities.

Protection against manipulators, false reports, and misuse

The system only works if it protects children and protects the process from misuse. Safe adults should be accountable, but the system must also reject manipulation, false reporting, and weaponised allegations.

POSH must not be used for personal disputes, revenge, harassment, reputation damage, false allegations, manipulation, family conflict, workplace conflict, social conflict, or any purpose unrelated to genuine child safety.

A person who misuses the system is acting against the safety purpose of POSH.

False or misleading information is a breach

If a POSH member knowingly provides false, misleading, malicious, reckless, exaggerated, incomplete, or manipulated information, that conduct may be treated as a breach of the Safe Adult Accountability Agreement.

Membership can be removed

If misuse or breach is found after review, the person may lose active POSH accountability status and be removed from the system.

Records may be preserved

Where lawful and appropriate, POSH may preserve the signed agreement, account records, reports, audit history, identity records, and supporting material.

Authorities may be notified

Serious misuse, false reporting, harassment, child-safety manipulation, or unlawful conduct may be referred to the proper authorities, safeguarding bodies, police, regulators, courts, or legal representatives.

Good-faith reporting remains protected.

POSH should never punish a person simply because a genuine concern cannot be proven. Action should only be considered where the information appears knowingly false, malicious, reckless, misleading, manipulated, or used to misuse the child-safety system.

Free checks should be the standard

Child-safety screening should not be blocked by cost. If a community expects adults to prove they are safe around children, then access to basic safety checks should not depend on whether someone can afford the fee.

No major financial barrier

The long-term position is that core child-safety checks should be free or government-funded so responsible adults can be checked without cost becoming a barrier.

More layers, not fewer

POSH status should sit beside official checks, not above them. A safer system uses multiple safeguards working together.

Accountability culture

Checks are not just paperwork. They should support a culture where safe adults expect transparency and unsafe behaviour becomes harder to hide.

Member verification concept

The future application should allow a person to prove their own active POSH accountability status without exposing private personal details or allowing open public searching.

POSH ID or QR code

Adults could choose to share a unique POSH ID or QR code with parents, organisations, clubs, or families to confirm their active status.

No open public name search

POSH should avoid letting people search anyone by name. Verification should happen through a shared POSH ID, QR code, or consent-based pathway.

Status only

The system should show status, not accusations. Example: “Active”, “Expired”, “Suspended pending review”, or “No active status.”

No public shaming

POSH should not publish allegations, criminal history, rumours, private reports, or public labels. Concerns must be handled through proper pathways.

Concern reporting pathway

If a concern is raised, POSH must not act like a court. POSH should preserve information, protect children, avoid defamation, prevent misuse, and direct serious matters to proper authorities.

Concern received

↓

Core fairness rule: a report does not automatically mean guilt.

POSH must separate child-safety urgency from public accusation. Children must be protected, but adults must not be publicly labelled without lawful process.

What removal would mean

Removal from the POSH system must be handled carefully.

Removed does not publicly mean “unsafe”, “guilty”, “offender”, or “criminal.”

Removed only means the person no longer holds active POSH accountability status.

If removal happens after a confirmed breach, the one-time $5 contribution should be refunded where lawful and practical so POSH does not profit from breach removal.

Serious concerns, misuse, evidence, and signed agreements should be directed to the appropriate authorities where lawful and necessary.

What this system must not become

Not a public offender register

POSH must not publish, recreate, expose, or attempt to replace any sex offender register.

Not a public accusation list

POSH must not become a place where people are publicly named, accused, attacked, shamed, or targeted.

Not proof of guilt

A report, refusal, expiry, suspension, or removal must not be treated publicly as proof that someone has committed an offence.

Not a tool for liars or manipulators

POSH must not allow people to use child-safety language to make knowingly false claims, manipulate disputes, damage reputations, or misuse safeguarding processes.

Who could use this?

Parents & carers

To check whether an adult has accepted POSH child-safety boundaries before babysitting, sleepovers, transport, coaching, mentoring, or regular child contact.

Babysitters & family friends

To show they are willing to operate with transparency, boundaries, visible communication, truthful information, and accountability.

Teachers & coaches

To support safer child-facing roles with clear expectations around communication, secrecy, grooming patterns, reporting, and conduct.

Clubs & organisations

To add a visible accountability layer beside existing safeguarding policies and official screening.

Safe adults

To show they take child safety seriously and are willing to be transparent about expectations.

Community networks

To help families build a stronger safety culture where adults understand the same rules and children know who they can talk to.

Build roadmap before the application

Before building the app, POSH needs the rules, documents, review process, privacy model, misuse protection, refund policy, and safety wording locked down.

Step 1 — Public concept page

Publish this page to explain the system, invite feedback, and make clear what POSH is and is not trying to build.

Step 2 — Agreement PDF

Create a clean Safe Adult Accountability Agreement with identity fields, declarations, child-safety boundaries, truthful information clause, misuse clause, signature, witness, and review wording.

Step 3 — Review pack

Prepare a legal, safeguarding, parent, organisation, and community feedback pack before allowing public sign-ups.

Step 4 — Privacy and data rules

Define what is stored, who can verify, what appears publicly, how removals work, how disputes work, how refunds work, and when authorities are notified.

Step 5 — Pilot program

Test the concept with a small trusted group of parents, safe adults, coaches, carers, sitters, safeguarding reviewers, and privacy-minded testers.

Step 6 — Application build

Build the first version with application forms, identity verification, agreement signing, one-time payment, POSH ID, QR verification, concern reporting, misuse review, and admin review.

Future application modules

Adult application

Identity details, declarations, agreement acceptance, digital signature, one-time $5 contribution, optional WWCC/police check reference, and renewal reminders if needed.

Status verification

POSH ID, QR code, consent-based verification, and status-only results.

Concern reporting

Structured reports, evidence preservation guidance, urgent-risk routing, private review, misuse screening, and authority referral prompts.

Admin review

Private case notes, document uploads, status changes, expiry, suspension, removal, refund records, and audit trail.

Organisation portal

Clubs, schools, groups, coaches, and community programs could manage verified adult participation.

Parent dashboard

Parents could save trusted adults, review child-safety boundaries, verify POSH IDs, and access reporting and conversation tools.

Suggested status language

The wording matters. POSH should use neutral status language that avoids public accusation.

Active

This person currently holds active POSH Safe Adult Accountability status.

Expired

This person previously held POSH status, but it has expired and has not been renewed.

Suspended pending review

This status is temporarily unavailable while POSH reviews information under its safeguarding and misuse-prevention process.

No active status

POSH cannot confirm an active accountability status for this person.

POSH should avoid public labels such as unsafe, dangerous, offender, banned, accused, predator, guilty, liar, false reporter, or flagged.

Review needed before launch

This system has potential, but it must be built carefully. The next step is review, not rushing straight into public sign-ups.

Legal review

Privacy, defamation, discrimination, data storage, consent, reporting obligations, refund policy, dispute handling, identity verification, and wording.

Safeguarding review

Child-safe standards, disclosure safety, mandatory reporting, grooming indicators, organisational use, escalation rules, and trauma-informed response.

Technical review

Secure database design, authentication, audit trails, admin permissions, encryption, identity checks, QR verification, abuse prevention, and report handling.

Community review

Parents, carers, safe adults, coaches, teachers, babysitters, and organisations should be able to test whether the wording is clear, fair, and hard to misuse.

Official POSH position

POSH supports more child-safety checks, free or government-funded access to core screening, stronger adult accountability, clearer safeguarding culture, misuse protection, and better reporting pathways.

POSH does not support public vigilantism, public accusation lists, exposing private registers, replacing proper legal systems, or allowing people to weaponise child-safety language for false or malicious purposes.

The goal is simple: make safe adults easier to identify, make boundaries clearer, make unsafe behaviour harder to hide, and make manipulation of the system harder to get away with.

Help shape this before the app is built
