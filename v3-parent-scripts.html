Parent Scripts

Calm words can keep a child talking. The right script can reduce fear, shame, and silence.

What parents say matters
CALM WORDS CREATE SAFE DISCLOSURE
When a child feels worried, ashamed, confused, or scared, the way a parent responds can either open the conversation or shut it down.
The goal is not to sound perfect.
The goal is to help your child feel safe enough to keep talking.

The first rule

Say less. Stay calm. Keep them talking.

Children often stop sharing when they sense blame, panic, anger, or punishment.

Safety first. Calm first. Listening first.

Best first things to say

“You’re not in trouble.”
↓
“I’m glad you told me.”
↓
“Thank you for being honest.”
↓
“We’ll work through this together.”
These phrases reduce fear and help children feel safer continuing the conversation.

If something feels off and you want to ask gently

  • “You seem a bit different lately. Is something bothering you?”
  • “Has anything online made you feel uncomfortable?”
  • “Is anyone talking to you in a way that feels weird or confusing?”
  • “You can tell me the truth, even if it feels awkward.”
  • “I’m here to help, not to get you in trouble.”
Open, calm questions are better than aggressive ones. Your child should feel invited, not interrogated.

If your child tells you something happened

  • “Thank you for telling me.”
  • “You did the right thing.”
  • “This is not your fault.”
  • “I believe you.”
  • “We’re going to handle this together.”
Reassurance matters. Many children fear they will be blamed more than they fear the original contact.

What not to say

  • “Why did you do that?”
  • “I told you not to talk to strangers.”
  • “What were you thinking?”
  • “You should have known better.”
  • “That was stupid.”
  • “You’ve lost your phone.”
Even if your fear comes out as anger, a child may hear it as blame and stop sharing important details.

Simple script flow for parents

Keep the conversation slow and clear.

Reassure first
↓
Ask one calm question at a time
↓
Let them explain in their own words
↓
Avoid blame or panic
↓
Preserve evidence and act safely
The more regulated you are, the safer your child will feel.

Scripts for younger children

  • “Did anyone say something online that made you feel yucky or confused?”
  • “Did anyone ask you to keep a secret from me?”
  • “Did anyone ask for pictures or private chats?”
  • “You can always tell me, even if someone said not to.”
Younger children often need simpler words. Focus on feelings, secrets, and whether someone asked for private contact.

Scripts for teens

  • “I’m not here to overreact. I just want to understand what’s going on.”
  • “If someone is pressuring you, manipulating you, or asking for secrecy, I want you to tell me.”
  • “You won’t be punished first for being honest with me.”
  • “Show me what happened and we’ll deal with it step by step.”
Teens often respond better when they feel respected, not treated like they are already guilty.

If your child shuts down or says “nothing”

  • “That’s okay. You don’t have to explain everything right now.”
  • “I’m here when you’re ready.”
  • “You can show me instead of saying it if that feels easier.”
  • “Whatever it is, we can deal with it together.”
Pressure can close the conversation. Calm presence often reopens it later.

If you need to ask to see the messages or device

  • “Can you show me what happened so I can help properly?”
  • “I’m not looking to punish you. I’m trying to understand the situation.”
  • “Let’s look at it together.”
Asking collaboratively usually works better than grabbing the device in anger.

If there are sexual messages, threats, or pressure

Move calmly, but treat it seriously.

Reassure the child, preserve evidence, stop contact if safe, and move to reporting quickly.

Serious sexual contact or coercion should always be treated urgently

Why this page matters

Parents do not need perfect words. They need safe words.

This page exists to help parents respond in ways that reduce fear, support disclosure, and protect children without pushing them further into silence.

Child safety improves when children feel safe telling the truth.
