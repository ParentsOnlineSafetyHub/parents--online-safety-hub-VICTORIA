Executive Functioning Application Layer

Knowing the skill is not enough.
Children need to practise using it in real online moments.

How to use this page:
This page turns executive functioning into daily habits, scripts, routines, and real-world safety practice.
Where thinking becomes behaviour
PRACTISE THE PAUSE UNTIL IT BECOMES AUTOMATIC
Children do not magically use good judgement under pressure. They need repeated practice in calm moments so safer thinking is available when the moment becomes emotional, exciting, confusing, or risky.
The goal is not perfect children.
The goal is children who can pause, think, ask for help, and recover before risk escalates.

The application rule

A skill only protects when the child can actually use it.

Use simple language. Practise often. Repeat calmly.

Executive functioning must become daily behaviour

The daily POSH method

Pause

This gives children a simple path when the online moment feels too fast.

1. Practise before the problem

Do not wait until your child is already upset, embarrassed, pressured, or hiding something.

  • Use calm moments to practise decisions
  • Talk through “what would you do?” examples
  • Make the pause normal before it is needed
  • Keep examples short and realistic
  • Repeat the same safety language often
The skill must be familiar before the risky moment arrives.

2. Use real online examples

  • Someone sends a friend request
  • Someone offers Robux, skins, gifts, or rewards
  • Someone asks to move to another app
  • Someone says “don’t tell your parents”
  • Someone makes your child feel guilty
  • Someone pressures them to reply quickly
Executive functioning works best when the examples match the world your child actually uses.

3. Build pause phrases

Children need simple phrases they can remember under pressure.

“I don’t have to answer straight away.”

“I can stop and think first.”

“If someone wants secrecy, I should tell an adult.”

“A gift does not mean I owe them anything.”

“If it feels weird, I can get help.”

Short phrases are easier to use when emotions are high.

4. Turn emotions into signals

Online risk often feels emotional before it looks obvious.

  • Excited can mean slow down
  • Embarrassed can mean tell someone
  • Scared can mean stop replying
  • Confused can mean ask for help
  • Pressured can mean pause immediately
Teach children that feelings are information — not instructions.

5. Use the “safe adult check”

This is one of the simplest protective habits.

If you would feel nervous showing a parent, pause.

If someone tells you not to show a parent, pause.

If you feel like you need to hide it, pause.

The 10-second reset

Teach this as a simple online safety reset.

Stop typing
↓
Put device down
↓
Take one breath
↓
Ask: “What is happening here?”
↓
Choose the safest next move
This helps interrupt fast reactions before they become unsafe decisions.

Daily parent prompts

“What would you do if someone online offered you something free?”
“What would you do if someone asked you not to tell me?”
“What does pressure feel like online?”
“When should you pause before replying?”
“Who are your safe adults if something feels wrong?”

Daily child habits

  • Pause before accepting new friend requests
  • Pause before replying to emotional messages
  • Pause before sharing personal information
  • Pause before accepting gifts
  • Pause when someone wants secrecy
  • Pause when something feels confusing or wrong
The repeated habit is simple: pause first, act second.

Use this with house rules

House rules should not just say what is banned. They should explain what thinking skill the child is practising.

Rule: No secret chats.

Skill: Recognising secrecy pressure.

Rule: Ask before accepting gifts.

Skill: Slowing down impulse decisions.

Rule: Devices charge outside bedrooms.

Skill: Reducing hidden late-night decisions.
