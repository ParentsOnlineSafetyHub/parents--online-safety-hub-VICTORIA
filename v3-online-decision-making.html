POSH

Online Decision Making

Good choices online are built before the pressure moment.
Children make safer decisions when they learn to pause, think, question, and choose with awareness.

Executive functioning skill:
Decision making helps children slow down, compare choices, understand consequences, and choose the safer path instead of reacting to pressure.
Think before choosing
A SAFE CHOICE STARTS WITH A PAUSE
Online pressure can move fast. A message, invite, gift, photo request, dare, or secret can feel urgent in the moment. Decision making helps children slow the moment down before they act.
The goal is not fear.
The goal is teaching children how to think clearly when something online feels confusing, exciting, risky, or wrong.

Why online decisions are harder for children

Children are still learning how to judge risk, delay reactions, and think through consequences.

Online platforms are designed to move fast, reward quick action, and keep attention locked in.

Fast online pressure can override slow careful thinking

What online decision making means

Online decision making is the ability to stop and ask:

  • Is this safe?
  • Why is this person asking me?
  • What could happen next?
  • Would I be okay showing this to a safe adult?
  • Am I being rushed, pressured, rewarded, or threatened?
  • What is the safest next step?
The best decision is not always the fastest decision.

The safer decision pattern

Notice the moment
↓
Pause before acting
↓
Ask what could happen next
↓
Choose the safer option
↓
Tell a safe adult if unsure
Children do not need perfect judgment. They need a simple repeatable pattern.

Common online moments where kids need this skill

  • Accepting friend requests from strangers
  • Replying to private messages
  • Joining unknown groups, chats, servers, or parties
  • Clicking links or downloading files
  • Sharing photos, videos, usernames, school details, or locations
  • Keeping secrets from parents or safe adults
  • Accepting gifts, Robux, skins, coins, or online rewards
  • Moving from one platform to another private app
These moments may look small, but they can become entry points for bigger risk.

The three-question check

1. Why me?
Why is this person asking me, choosing me, or giving me attention?

2. What next?
What might happen if I say yes, reply, click, send, or keep this secret?

3. Who knows?
Would a safe adult know about this, or am I being pushed to hide it?

If the answer involves secrecy, pressure, fear, or guilt — stop and tell someone.

Decision making red flags

A child needs to pause and get help when someone online says things like:

  • “Don’t tell your parents.”
  • “It will be our secret.”
  • “You can trust me.”
  • “I’ll give you something if you do this.”
  • “You’ll get in trouble if you tell.”
  • “Send it quickly.”
  • “Delete the messages.”
  • “Move to another app.”
Safe people do not need secret pressure to get a child to cooperate.

Pressure changes decisions

Children can make choices they would not normally make when they feel special, scared, rushed, guilty, embarrassed, or threatened.

This is why calm preparation matters before the pressure moment happens.

The more pressure there is, the more important the pause becomes.

How parents can teach this without lecturing

  • Talk through pretend situations before real ones happen.
  • Use short questions instead of long warnings.
  • Ask “what would you do next?” during everyday app conversations.
  • Teach that unsure means stop, screenshot, and ask.
  • Reward honesty instead of punishing the first disclosure.
  • Keep repeating the same safety standards calmly.
Children learn safer decisions through repetition, not one serious talk.

Parent script

“You do not have to decide everything by yourself online. If something feels weird, rushed, secret, scary, or confusing, pause and show me. You will not be in trouble for asking for help.”
“A safe person will never need you to hide messages from the adults who protect you.”
“If someone offers you something online, asks for something back, or makes you feel like you owe them, stop and check with me first.”

Child script

Children can use simple words when they are unsure:

“I need to check with my parent first.”

“I do not keep secrets online.”

“I am not sending that.”

“I am leaving this chat.”

“Something feels weird and I need help.”

Simple words are easier to use under pressure.

The POSH decision rule

If it feels rushed
↓
If it feels secret
↓
If it feels uncomfortable
↓
If it involves gifts or pressure
↓
Pause and tell a safe adult

How this links to executive functioning

  • Impulse control: stopping before reacting
  • Emotional regulation: not letting fear or excitement take over
  • Working memory: remembering the safety rule in the moment
  • Flexible thinking: choosing another option instead of following pressure
  • Self-monitoring: noticing when something feels wrong
Stronger executive functioning gives children more space between pressure and action.

Final reminder

Children do not become safer by being scared of the internet.

They become safer by learning how to think, pause, question, and ask for help when something does not feel right.

Better decisions begin with calmer thinking.
