Self Harm & Suicide Warning Signs

Some signs are quiet, not obvious.
Knowing what to look for can help you act before it becomes a crisis.

Take it seriously early
EARLY SUPPORT CAN SAVE LIVES
Children and teens do not always say they are struggling directly. Changes in behaviour, mood, or communication can be early signals that something deeper is going on.
If something feels off, trust your instinct.
It is always better to check early than to wait.

This is not about blame

Children who struggle are not weak.

Parents who miss early signs are not failures.

What matters most is recognising it and acting early

Warning signs parents should look for

  • Sudden withdrawal from family and friends
  • Talking about feeling hopeless, worthless, or trapped
  • Loss of interest in things they used to enjoy
  • Changes in sleep (too much or too little)
  • Changes in appetite or energy levels
  • Increased secrecy or isolation
  • Giving away possessions or saying goodbye in unusual ways
  • Talking about death, disappearing, or “not being here”
  • Self-harm behaviours (cuts, burns, covering skin)
  • Strong emotional swings or sudden calm after distress
One sign alone may not mean crisis — but multiple signs together should never be ignored.

Things children might say

  • “I’m tired of everything.”
  • “Nothing matters anymore.”
  • “You’d be better off without me.”
  • “I just want everything to stop.”
  • “I don’t see the point anymore.”
Even indirect comments should always be taken seriously.

Online factors that can increase risk

  • Exposure to harmful content or communities
  • Cyberbullying or social pressure
  • Isolation through private chats or secret groups
  • Emotional manipulation or control by others
  • Comparison culture and unrealistic expectations
Online influence can intensify emotions and isolation without parents seeing it directly.

How risk can build

Stress, pressure, or emotional pain
↓
Withdrawal or isolation
↓
Negative thinking patterns
↓
Hopelessness or distress
↓
Self-harm or suicidal thoughts

What parents should do immediately

  • Stay calm and present
  • Listen without interrupting or judging
  • Take every concern seriously
  • Ask directly if they are okay or thinking about harming themselves
  • Remove immediate risks if needed (objects, isolation)
  • Stay close and do not leave them alone if you are concerned
  • Seek professional support immediately
Asking directly does not put the idea in their head — it opens a door for them to speak.

Get help immediately

If you believe your child is in immediate danger, call emergency services.

You do not have to handle this alone.

Call Emergency (000) Lifeline (13 11 14) Kids Helpline (1800 55 1800)

Keep communication open

Children are more likely to speak when they feel safe, not judged.

Your presence, calmness, and support matter more than perfect words.
