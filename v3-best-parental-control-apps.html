Best Parental Control Apps

No app replaces parenting. But the right tool can give parents more visibility, better control, and earlier warning signs.

How to use this page:
Start with the device your child uses most.
Built-in controls are usually the best first move.
Add stronger monitoring only if your family needs more visibility, alerts, or filtering.

Start with the truth

Parental control apps can help with supervision, limits, app approvals, and visibility.

They do not replace calm conversations, house rules, or understanding how risk actually works.

A monitoring app can support safety, but it cannot build trust by itself

Best place to start for most families

Built-in tools are often the best place to start before paying for more advanced monitoring.

Good options parents often use

  • Google Family Link — strong starting point for Android families
  • Apple Screen Time — best first step for iPhone and iPad families
  • Microsoft Family Safety — useful for Windows, Xbox, and Microsoft-based households
  • Qustodio — popular for families wanting stronger cross-device filtering and monitoring
  • Bark — often chosen by parents wanting alerts around messages, social platforms, and online behaviour
  • Norton Family — useful for time limits, web supervision, search visibility, and general oversight
The “best” parental control app depends more on your devices, your child’s age, and the level of visibility you need than on the name alone.

What built-in tools are good at

  • Managing screen time and downtime
  • Approving or blocking app installs and purchases
  • Applying content restrictions and age settings
  • Viewing app use patterns and time spent
  • Keeping structure around device use without adding another paid service
For many families, built-in controls handle the basics well enough to reduce risk immediately.

When parents usually look for stronger apps

  • When they want alerts instead of just time limits
  • When the child uses multiple devices and platforms
  • When social media, messaging, or secrecy is becoming a concern
  • When web filtering and activity reports need to go deeper
  • When one built-in system does not cover the whole household

What these apps are good for

  • Managing screen time
  • Approving or blocking app installs
  • Viewing device use patterns
  • Setting time limits and downtime
  • Reducing exposure to unsuitable content
  • Helping parents keep structure around device use
  • In some cases, getting alerts about higher-risk behaviour or content

What they are not good at

  • Stopping all manipulation or grooming
  • Understanding emotional risk on their own
  • Replacing real conversations
  • Preventing secrecy if the child uses other devices, other accounts, or workarounds
  • Making parents automatically aware of the bigger pattern
A strong app can show activity. It cannot always explain what that activity means.

Simple way to choose the right one

Match it to your device type first

Decide whether you need basic controls or deeper monitoring

Keep the setup simple enough that you will actually use it

Focus on consistency, not just features

The best app is usually the one that fits your devices, your rules, and your child’s real behaviour online.

Important reminder

What works better is a combination of settings, structure, trust, and early action when behaviour changes.

Visibility matters most when it is paired with understanding
