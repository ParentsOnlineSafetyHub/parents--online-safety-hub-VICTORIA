Grandparents & Older Generations

Grandparents Still Protect Children — The World Just Changed.

Children are growing up inside games, group chats, apps, private messages, livestreams, videos, disappearing content, and online friendships. Many grandparents did not grow up with this world, but they still understand something very important: children need safe adults who notice when something feels wrong.

You do not need to become a technology expert to help protect your grandchildren.
You only need to understand the patterns, the warning signs, and the calm words that keep a child talking.

Why grandparents matter in online safety

Grandparents often hold a special place in a child’s life. A child may be scared to tell a parent because they fear getting in trouble, losing their device, being embarrassed, or upsetting the family. But sometimes they will speak more openly to a grandparent because the relationship feels calmer, softer, and less immediate.

Grandparents notice changes

You may notice when a child becomes withdrawn, unusually secretive, anxious around their phone, tired from late-night use, or emotionally attached to someone online. These changes may not prove danger, but they are worth paying attention to.

Grandparents have life experience

Even if the technology feels new, the human behaviour is not. Trust, secrecy, pressure, manipulation, flattery, gifts, shame, and fear have always existed. The internet has simply given those behaviours new places to happen.

Grandparents can stay calm

A calm grandparent can help a child feel safe enough to speak. The first response matters. If the child feels blamed or panicked at, they may stop talking. If they feel believed and protected, they are more likely to tell the truth.

The technology changed. The warning signs did not. Children still need trusted adults who can recognise secrecy, pressure, fear, confusion, and sudden behaviour changes.

The online world explained in plain language

Many adults think children are simply “playing games” or “watching videos.” But for children, the online world can also be where they socialise, make friends, receive attention, compare themselves, hide problems, join groups, and speak privately with people their family may not know.

Games are not always just games

Many games now include chat, friend requests, voice conversations, private groups, gifting, usernames, avatars, and links to other apps. A child may start in a game like Roblox, Fortnite, Minecraft, VRChat, or another multiplayer game, then be encouraged to move the conversation somewhere more private.

Grandparents do not need to memorise every game. The important question is: can other people contact the child, talk privately, offer gifts, or move the child to another app?

Apps can hide conversations

Apps like Discord, Snapchat, Instagram, TikTok, Telegram, WhatsApp, and other messaging platforms can allow children to speak privately, join groups, receive messages, or view content quickly. Some apps can make conversations harder for parents to see because messages disappear, accounts are hidden, or chats move between platforms.

The app name matters less than the pattern: private contact, secrecy, emotional pressure, gifts, deleted messages, or fear of telling an adult.

Algorithms keep children watching

Many platforms are designed to keep people scrolling, watching, clicking, reacting, and returning. Children can be shown more of what catches their attention, even if that content is stressful, inappropriate, confusing, addictive, or emotionally intense.

A grandparent might notice a child becoming irritated when asked to stop, constantly needing stimulation, copying online behaviour, or becoming upset by content they do not fully understand.

Online friendships can feel very real

Children may form strong emotional attachments to people online. Sometimes these friendships are harmless. Other times, the relationship becomes risky when the person asks for secrecy, private chats, photos, personal information, money, gifts, or loyalty against family.

A child may say, “They understand me better than anyone,” or “You do not know them like I do.” That does not automatically mean danger, but it is a sign adults should slow things down and look closer.

Old risks in new places

The internet has not invented every danger from scratch. It has changed where old dangers can happen. A stranger does not need to stand outside a school gate anymore. A manipulative person may now appear inside a game, a group chat, a comment section, a livestream, a private message, or a friend request.

Attention

A child may receive compliments, praise, emotional support, or special attention from someone online. Attention can feel powerful to a child, especially if they feel lonely, misunderstood, bored, anxious, or left out.

Gifts

Gifts may be digital, such as Robux, skins, game items, subscriptions, boosts, or in-game currency. A gift can create a feeling of debt, loyalty, or secrecy, even when the child does not understand that pressure.

Secrecy

Secrecy is one of the most important warning signs. A risky person may encourage a child not to tell parents, delete messages, hide accounts, move apps, or say “your family would not understand.”

Grandparent translation: if someone online is trying to become more important than the family, more private than the family, or more trusted than the family, slow the situation down.

Common apps and games explained for grandparents

This is not a complete list, and the names will keep changing. The goal is to help grandparents understand what these platforms can be used for and what questions to ask.

Roblox

Roblox can look like a children’s game, but it is also a platform full of different games, usernames, avatars, chat features, friend requests, groups, and digital currency called Robux. Some children use it safely. Others may be exposed to strangers, pressure, scams, gifting, or private communication.

Grandparent question: “Who do you play with on Roblox, and do you know them in real life?”

Discord

Discord is like a private clubroom connected to gaming and online communities. Children may join servers, voice chats, group chats, and private messages. It can be useful for friends, but it can also move conversations away from where parents can easily see them.

Grandparent question: “Are you in any Discord groups with people your family does not know?”

Snapchat

Snapchat is built around quick messages, photos, videos, streaks, and disappearing content. Some children like it because it feels private and fast. That can also make it harder for adults to understand what has been said, especially if a child is being pressured.

Grandparent question: “Has anyone ever asked you to send something on Snapchat because it disappears?”

TikTok and YouTube

TikTok and YouTube can be used for entertainment, learning, humour, music, and hobbies. They can also expose children to trends, comments, livestreams, influencers, adult topics, unrealistic comparisons, and endless scrolling.

Grandparent question: “What kind of videos keep coming up for you lately?”

Instagram and social media

Instagram and similar platforms involve photos, videos, private messages, followers, comments, stories, and reels. Children may compare themselves to others, receive unwanted messages, or feel pressure around appearance, popularity, and attention.

Grandparent question: “Do people you do not know ever message you or comment on your photos?”

Gaming voice chat

Many online games allow players to talk using headsets. A child may be speaking to friends, older teenagers, or adults while playing. Voice chat can feel casual, but it can also allow pressure, bullying, insults, grooming, or private invitations.

Grandparent question: “When you are wearing the headset, who can hear you and who are you talking to?”

Warning signs grandparents may notice

One warning sign does not prove that something dangerous is happening. Children can be moody, private, tired, embarrassed, or stressed for many reasons. But several signs together mean it is time for calm attention.

Behaviour changes

Watch for a child becoming suddenly anxious, withdrawn, angry, secretive, unusually tired, or overly defensive about their phone, tablet, console, or online friends.

The important part is not to accuse. The safer approach is to gently ask what has changed and whether something online is making them feel stressed.

Device secrecy

A child may turn the screen away, close apps quickly, delete messages, use a device late at night, become distressed when asked to stop, or become protective of a private chat.

Privacy is normal as children grow, but secrecy mixed with fear, pressure, or panic needs adult attention.

New online attachment

A child may talk constantly about one online friend, defend that person strongly, become upset when unable to contact them, or act as if the online person understands them better than family.

Online friendships are not automatically bad. The concern rises when the relationship becomes secret, intense, controlling, or isolated from family awareness.

Pressure or fear

A child may seem frightened of a message, worried about screenshots, scared someone will expose them, or anxious that they have done something wrong online.

This is when calm adult response becomes critical. Shame can make children hide. Calm support can help them tell the truth.

What to say to a grandchild

The words you use matter. A child may test whether it is safe to tell you by giving you a small piece of the story first. If your reaction is calm, they may tell you more. If your reaction is anger, panic, blame, or threats, they may shut down.

Start soft

“I do not need to understand the whole app straight away. I just want to understand what has been happening and how it made you feel.”

This tells the child they do not need to explain the entire internet before they are believed.

Remove blame

“You are not in trouble for telling me. If someone has made you uncomfortable, pressured you, or scared you, we can slow it down together.”

Children often hide because they think adults will blame them first. Removing blame helps them speak.

Ask about pressure

“Has anyone asked you to keep a secret, delete messages, move to another app, send a photo, or not tell your parents?”

This helps identify the pattern without accusing the child of doing something wrong.

Keep the door open

“You can come back to me even if you are embarrassed, even if you clicked something, even if you replied, and even if you think you made a mistake.”

Many children need to hear that one mistake does not mean they are bad or beyond help.

Best grandparent rule: listen first, thank them for telling you, stay calm, then help them bring in the right safe adult if the situation needs action.

What not to do first

When something feels wrong, it is natural to want to grab the phone, demand answers, delete everything, or confront someone. But the first reaction can affect whether the child keeps talking and whether important evidence is preserved.

Do not shame the child

Avoid saying things like “How could you be so silly?” or “Why would you do that?” Shame can make a child hide more and tell less.

Do not threaten everything first

If the first response is “You are losing your phone forever,” the child may decide honesty is dangerous. Safety rules may still be needed, but calm comes first.

Do not delete evidence

If threats, blackmail, sexual requests, grooming, or dangerous contact are involved, messages, usernames, screenshots, links, and account details may matter.

How grandparents can help parents without taking over

Grandparents can be a steady support without turning the situation into a family argument. The goal is not to criticise the parent or accuse the child. The goal is to help the family see what is happening and take the next right step.

Be a calm witness

If you notice changes, write down what you observed without exaggerating it. For example: “She seemed very upset after checking Snapchat,” or “He closed the laptop quickly when I walked in.” Calm observations are more useful than panic.

Support the family rules

Children can become confused if parents set rules but grandparents ignore them. Ask the parents what the device rules are around bedtime, bedrooms, gaming, downloads, online friends, and private messaging.

Ask before investigating

Unless there is immediate danger, avoid secretly going through a child’s device without family discussion. A better role may be helping the child feel safe enough to talk and helping the parent respond calmly.

Help with routines

Grandparents can help children build offline balance: meals without screens, device-free visits, outdoor time, hobbies, reading, cooking, chores, and ordinary conversation. These simple routines reduce the hold that online life can have.

Simple device and home safety habits

Grandparents may not control the child’s main device settings, but they can still help create safer habits when children visit, stay overnight, or use devices in their home.

Keep devices in shared spaces when possible, especially for younger children.
Avoid unsupervised late-night device use in bedrooms during sleepovers.
Ask parents what apps, games, and platforms the child is allowed to use.
Do not allow unknown adults or strangers to speak privately with the child through games or apps.
Encourage the child to tell you if anyone asks them to keep a secret online.
Teach the child that screenshots, usernames, and messages should not be deleted if someone is threatening them.
Make sure the child knows they can ask for help without being shamed.

A simple grandparent rule: no secret online friendships, no private messages with unknown adults, no late-night hidden device use, and no shame for telling the truth.

When grandparents should move faster

Some situations need calm action now. Do not wait for perfect proof if the child may be in danger.

Move faster if there are threats, blackmail, sexual requests, requests for photos, pressure to keep secrets, talk about meeting in person, fear that images will be shared, or a child saying they feel trapped.

Do not shame. Do not panic. Do not delete evidence first. Slow the contact down and get help.

Grandparent scripts

These are simple phrases grandparents can use. They are designed to keep the child talking, not make them feel interrogated.

If the child seems upset after using a device

“I noticed you seemed a bit different after using your phone. I am not angry. I just want to check if something online made you feel uncomfortable.”

If the child is hiding the screen

“I know everyone likes privacy, but when screens start feeling secret or stressful, it is usually a sign we should talk gently about it.”

If the child says parents will be angry

“We can tell them carefully. The first job is to keep you safe. You telling the truth is a good thing, even if the situation feels messy.”

If the child says it was their fault

“Children can be pressured online by people who know how to make things confusing. You are not bad for needing help.”

If someone asked for secrecy

“Safe people do not need children to keep unsafe secrets from family. If someone asked you to hide something, we should look at that together.”

If the child is scared

“You do not have to handle this by yourself. We can slow it down, keep the evidence, and get the right help one step at a time.”

The POSH message for older generations

You grew up in a world where danger was easier to picture. A stranger at the park. A strange car near school. A person asking too many questions. A child acting scared or secretive.

Today, some of those same warning signs appear through phones, games, apps, usernames, avatars, disappearing messages, private groups, and online friendships.

You do not need to know every button. You do not need to understand every trend. You do not need to speak like a teenager.

You just need to stay calm, notice patterns, ask safe questions, and help children feel protected instead of ashamed.
