Talking With Kids

Kids talk when they feel safe.

The Most Important Rule

Your child must believe this: “You will never be in trouble for telling me something that happened online.”

Children often hide online problems because they fear losing their devices or getting blamed. If they fear punishment, they stay silent.

Safety comes before punishment.

When your child tells you something

  • Stay calm — even if you feel angry or scared.
  • Thank them for telling you.
  • Tell them they are not in trouble.
  • Listen before asking lots of questions.
  • Focus on solving the problem together.
Your reaction determines whether your child tells you the next time.

Ages 6–8

Young children need very simple rules.

  • Only talk to people you know in real life.
  • If someone asks for photos → tell a parent.
  • If someone asks to move to another app → tell a parent.
  • Never share your name, school, or address.
Example conversation: “Sometimes strangers pretend to be kids online. If anyone ever makes you uncomfortable, you can always tell me and you won't be in trouble.”

Ages 9–12

This is one of the most targeted age groups online.

  • Explain that people online may lie about their age.
  • Talk about private messaging risks.
  • Explain why predators move chats off platforms.
  • Teach them to screenshot and tell a parent.
  • Explain that gifts in games can be used to build trust.

Teenagers

Teens value independence. Focus on awareness instead of control.

  • Discuss manipulation and grooming tactics.
  • Talk about sextortion and blackmail risks.
  • Explain why predators target teens emotionally.
  • Encourage open communication instead of punishment.
  • Remind them they can ask for help even if they made a mistake.

Keep the conversation going

  • Have small conversations regularly.
  • Ask about games and apps they use.
  • Stay curious rather than suspicious.
  • Make safety discussions normal.
One calm conversation today can prevent a bigger problem later.
