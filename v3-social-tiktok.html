Why TikTok matters

TikTok can expose children to strangers, harmful trends, sexualised content, live interactions, and emotionally manipulative content very quickly.

Even passive scrolling teaches the algorithm what to show next, which means a child can be shaped by what they watch long before they understand why it keeps appearing.

A few clicks can change a child’s feed fast
Child Safety First:
TikTok is not only about who contacts a child. It is also about what repeatedly reaches them, what gets normalised, and how fast the algorithm can pull them deeper into certain content patterns.

Why TikTok creates high risk

  • The algorithm learns quickly from watch time, pauses, likes, and replays.
  • Content can shift from harmless entertainment into sexualised, extreme, manipulative, or fear-based material very fast.
  • Live streams, comments, and DMs can open pathways to stranger contact.
  • Children can be influenced emotionally even without actively searching for harmful content.
  • Repeated exposure can shape beliefs, attention, self-image, and behaviour over time.
The risk is not only one bad video. It is the ongoing feed pattern that keeps teaching the child what to watch next.

Important TikTok settings

1) Set the account to Private

2) Restrict direct messages

3) Disable or limit duets, stitches, and downloads

4) Use family pairing where possible

5) Review who follows and comments

6) Treat live features carefully or disable them where possible

TikTok safety is not just account privacy. It is also about reducing how much the child can be contacted, copied, drawn into trends, or shaped by the feed.

What parents should know about current teen settings

TikTok says accounts for users aged 13 to 15 are private by default, and accounts for users aged 16 to 17 are also private by default. TikTok also says direct messaging is not available for ages 13 to 15, while older teens can manage DM settings depending on age and privacy choices. Family Pairing gives parents and guardians added control over teen safety settings. 1

Safer defaults help, but they do not remove the need for checks, conversations, and clear family rules.

What begins as casual scrolling can become something much deeper.

A child does not need to search for harmful content directly. The algorithm can bring it to them.

Major red flags on TikTok

  • Older users messaging children
  • Pressure to move to Instagram, Snapchat, or Telegram
  • Live videos with unsafe comments or private invites
  • Sexualised trends or risky challenge content
  • Rapid change in the type of content appearing in the feed
  • Children becoming emotionally absorbed in one style of content or one creator
One of the clearest warning signs is when a child’s feed changes quickly and they become defensive or secretive about what they are watching.

What parents should do

  • Use private settings and Family Pairing.
  • Review what kind of content is appearing in the feed.
  • Watch for fast shifts into more mature, sexualised, fear-based, or emotionally manipulative material.
  • Talk with children about how algorithms shape what they see.
  • Make the off-platform rule very clear if contact starts through TikTok.
TikTok should be treated as both an exposure platform and a contact-risk platform.

Questions parents should ask

“What kind of videos keep showing up for you?”

“Have you noticed your feed getting more intense or weird?”

“Has anyone tried to message you or move you somewhere else?”

“Do you know why TikTok keeps showing you certain content?”

“Would you tell me if a live, comment, or DM started feeling off?”

The better question is not just “What are you watching?” It is “What is the feed teaching you to keep watching?”
