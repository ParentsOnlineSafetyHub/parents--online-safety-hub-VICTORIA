Predator Playbook

Predators follow patterns.
If you understand the pattern, you can stop it early.

Grooming usually does not start with something obvious. It often starts with attention, kindness, gifts, jokes, gaming, private messages, or someone seeming helpful and safe. This page shows the common escalation path so parents can recognise it earlier and interrupt it before the situation deepens.

This is how it actually happens
GROOMING IS A PROCESS — NOT A MOMENT
It rarely starts obvious. It starts normal, friendly, helpful, and easy to dismiss — then slowly moves into private spaces, secrecy, and control.
The biggest red flag:
Moving a child from public space → private communication

The Grooming Escalation Path

Grooming happens step by step — not all at once.

Each step reduces visibility and increases control.

The earlier you interrupt the move from public space into private space, the easier it usually is to stop the pattern.

How the pattern usually feels to a child

To adults, the pattern may look suspicious. To a child, it may feel like friendship, attention, support, gifts, praise, or someone who “gets them.”

“They’re just being nice.”

“They understand me better than other people.”

“They told me not to tell because people wouldn’t understand.”

“They said this is private, not bad.”

Children often do not recognise grooming while it is happening. Confusion is common.

What parents should notice early

  • Step 1: repeated contact from the same person
  • Step 2: moving into private messages
  • Step 3: voice chat, private groups, or “special access”
  • Step 4: secrecy, disappearing messages, or hidden apps
  • Step 5: pressure, shame, threats, control, or sexual content
You do not need every sign before taking safer action.

If a child cannot explain what’s happening

Children often do not have the right words. They may only seem confused, distant, guilty, pressured, or emotionally attached to someone they cannot explain properly.

A child does not need the right words to be taken seriously.

How parents break the pattern

  • Restrict DMs and private messaging where possible
  • Friends list = known people only where appropriate
  • No secret accounts
  • No moving to Discord, Snapchat, Telegram, or private apps with strangers
  • Regular short safety check-ins
  • Keep the child feeling safe enough to talk without panic
