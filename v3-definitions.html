Understanding Key Terms

Clear definitions help remove confusion, reduce fear, and make it easier to recognise real risk.

Words matter when understanding risk
CLARITY HELPS PROTECT
Many people hear these terms but are unsure what they actually mean in real life. Understanding behaviour is more important than labels.
This page is about awareness — not accusation.
Focus on patterns of behaviour, not guessing who someone is.

What is a predator?

A predator is someone who intentionally targets another person in order to gain control, trust, or exploitation.

  • They often build trust first.
  • They may appear friendly, supportive, or understanding.
  • They look for emotional vulnerability or opportunity.
  • They gradually introduce control, secrecy, or pressure.
A predator is defined by behaviour — not appearance.

What is a paedophile?

A paedophile is someone who has a sexual interest in children.

Not all individuals who harm children openly identify themselves, and many will hide this through manipulation and grooming behaviour.

  • They may attempt to normalise inappropriate behaviour.
  • They may build emotional trust before introducing sexual content.
  • They often rely on secrecy and control.
The risk comes from behaviour and actions — not labels alone.

Who is a victim?

A victim is someone who has been manipulated, pressured, or harmed — even if they did not realise it at the time.

  • A child who trusted someone and was misled.
  • A young person who felt pressured into sharing images or information.
  • Someone who was told to keep secrets or felt unable to speak up.
Being a victim does not mean weakness — it means someone took advantage of trust.

What is grooming?

Grooming is a process where someone builds trust with a child in order to manipulate or exploit them over time.

Grooming is often slow, subtle, and hard to recognise early.

What is manipulation?

Manipulation is when someone influences another person’s thoughts, emotions, or actions for their own benefit.

  • Creating guilt (“you’ll get me in trouble”)
  • Building dependency (“I’m the only one who understands you”)
  • Encouraging secrecy (“don’t tell anyone”)
  • Shifting blame (“this is your fault too”)
Manipulation often feels like care or support at the beginning.

If you are unsure about your situation

You do not need to be certain to ask for help or take steps to protect yourself.

  • Does someone ask you to keep secrets from your family?
  • Do you feel pressured, uncomfortable, or confused after talking to them?
  • Do they try to isolate you or make you depend on them?
  • Do they ask for things you don’t want to give?
If something feels wrong, that feeling matters.
