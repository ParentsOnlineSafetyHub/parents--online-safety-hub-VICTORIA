Real Scenarios

Sometimes risk does not look obvious at first. These scenarios help parents recognise common patterns and choose the right next step.

Why this page matters

Many parents do not recognise online risk because it often starts looking small, harmless, or easy to dismiss.

These examples are here to help you spot patterns earlier and respond with more clarity.

Most online harm starts with a pattern, not one obvious moment
Important:
These are common examples, not proof on their own. The value is in recognising the pattern early and taking the right next step.
If this feels familiar:
Do not ignore it. Use the scenario that feels closest, recognise the pattern, and move into the next step early.

1. “Someone offered my child Robux, gifts, or special help”

This often looks friendly at first. Someone offers in-game currency, gifts, help, rare items, boosts, or extra attention.

On its own, it may look harmless. But gifts are often used to build trust, create obligation, or open the door to private conversations.

Pattern to watch: gift → trust → private chat → secrecy
Best next move: Check the platform, understand the grooming pattern, and set a clear rule around gifts and private contact.

2. “Someone asked to move the chat to Discord, Snapchat, Telegram, or another app”

This is one of the clearest escalation points. Moving a conversation off-platform usually gives the other person more privacy, more control, and less visibility.

Children often do not realise this is a red flag because it can sound casual or normal.

Pattern to watch: public contact → private platform → less visibility → more pressure
Best next move: Treat this as escalation. Strengthen rules, preserve anything already said, and move into action earlier rather than later.

3. “My child is suddenly hiding their screen or turning devices away”

Screen hiding does not automatically mean something dangerous is happening, but it often signals secrecy, discomfort, fear of being seen, or a shift in behaviour that matters.

What matters most is whether this behaviour is new, frequent, emotionally charged, or linked to certain apps, times, or people.

Pattern to watch: secrecy + mood change + device defensiveness
Best next move: Stay calm, do not accuse, and use behaviour plus pattern-based pages before jumping to conclusions.

4. “My child suddenly has a second account or an account I did not know about”

Hidden or second accounts can be used to create distance from parents, avoid oversight, or continue conversations away from the main account.

Sometimes children create extra accounts to feel independent. Sometimes it is linked to secrecy, pressure, fear, or contact they do not want seen.

Pattern to watch: hidden account + secrecy + emotional reaction when asked
Best next move: Rebuild visibility without creating panic. Focus on safety, account access, and calm conversation first.

5. “They say it is just an online friend”

This is one of the most common ways risk is minimised. The other person may seem funny, kind, supportive, helpful, or the same age.

The problem is that children often judge intent by tone, not by risk pattern.

Pattern to watch: fast closeness + private contact + emotional dependence
Best next move: Focus less on what the person seems like and more on what the pattern is doing.

6. “My child seems anxious, withdrawn, angry, or emotionally different after being online”

Behaviour changes can matter even when there is no clear explanation yet. Some children show risk through mood, shutdown, agitation, sleep changes, defensiveness, or sudden attachment to a device.

The child may not understand what is happening well enough to explain it clearly.

Pattern to watch: emotional shift + device attachment + secrecy or avoidance
Best next move: Use calm conversation and definition-based pages first, then move into action if the pattern becomes clearer.
Understanding What’s Happening Red Flags Parent Scripts What To Do Now

7. “It looks like only videos or content, not direct contact”

Not all online harm starts with direct messaging. Sometimes risk comes through repeated exposure to manipulative, sexualised, extreme, or algorithm-driven content.

A child may be affected by what they are being shown long before there is obvious direct contact.

Pattern to watch: repeated exposure + recommendation drift + behaviour change
Best next move: Treat repeated exposure seriously. Content and algorithm pressure can still change behaviour, fear, and vulnerability.

8. “There are sexual messages, threats, blackmail, or pressure”

This is no longer a low-level concern. If sexual messaging, threats, blackmail, image pressure, or coercion are involved, the situation may require faster evidence preservation and reporting.

The child needs calm support, not blame.

Pattern to watch: fear + secrecy + pressure + control
Best next move: Move quickly into safety, evidence preservation, and reporting pathways. Do not delay because the situation already feels serious.
