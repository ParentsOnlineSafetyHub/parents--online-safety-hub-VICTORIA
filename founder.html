About the Founder

Jinglez – Founder of POSH Aussie

POSH Aussie was created by Graeme Ingle, known to many friends and online communities as Jinglez.

Jinglez is an Australian father, carpenter, gamer, and community builder who believes that protecting children begins with empowering parents with knowledge, awareness, and open conversations.

POSH stands for Parents Online Safety Hub — a platform created to help parents understand the digital environments their children are growing up in and how to guide them safely.

POSH Aussie was not created by government departments or large organisations. It was built independently by a parent who wanted to create practical tools that other families could actually use.

A Family-Focused Life

At the centre of everything Jinglez does is family.

He is part of a large blended family surrounded by children of many different ages, currently ranging from young toddlers through to pre-teens and teenagers who see both him and his fiancée as parental figures in their lives.

Some of the children are his own, some are step-children, and others are young people who have become part of their extended family circle over time.

For privacy and safety reasons, personal details about the children are intentionally kept minimal. What matters most is that POSH Aussie is built from real experience raising and caring for children in today's digital world.

Like many families in Australia, Jinglez and his family have also experienced interactions with child protection systems such as DFFH. While the details of those experiences remain private, they reinforced a powerful belief:

Children deserve safe environments, honest guidance, and strong families supporting them.

A Builder by Trade

For more than 16 years, Jinglez has worked as a carpenter and builder across Victoria.

Builders naturally look for solutions to problems. When something isn't working, you don't just complain about it — you design something better and build it.

POSH Aussie was created with that same mindset.

Instead of simply raising awareness about online safety risks, Jinglez wanted to create practical tools that parents could actually use.

The Positive Side of Online Communities

The internet is often portrayed only as a dangerous place for children, but it can also build positive friendships and communities when used responsibly.

Jinglez met his fiancée through online gaming while playing Fortnite, and several of his closest friendships today began years earlier through Call of Duty online gaming communities.

Those friendships eventually moved into the real world — including a memorable trip where friends met up and camped together at Kangaroo Lake in Victoria, spending time fishing, wakeboarding and enjoying the outdoors.

Experiences like this highlight an important message POSH promotes:

The internet itself isn't the enemy. Lack of awareness and guidance is.

Why POSH Was Created

Many safety campaigns tell parents to “talk to your kids about online safety”.

But very few actually explain:

  • What to say
  • When to say it
  • How conversations should change as children grow
  • How to approach difficult topics safely

That gap is exactly why POSH Aussie was created.

One of the most popular resources on the platform is the POSH Parent Scripts, which help parents start age-appropriate conversations with their children about online safety.

Independent and Community Driven

POSH Aussie is an entirely independent project.

It is not government funded and not backed by large institutions. Everything has been built from the ground up by Jinglez with the goal of helping families navigate online safety together.

The Bigger Vision

POSH Aussie is only the beginning.

The long-term vision includes expanding community resources such as:

  • Family play and visitation centres for parents and children
  • Community support networks for families facing challenges
  • Innovative housing ideas including container homes to help address homelessness

All of these ideas share the same core belief:

Strong families create strong communities.
