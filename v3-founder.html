Who built POSH

POSH was created by Graeme Ingle, known to many as Jinglez.

It was built independently by a father, carpenter, gamer, and community-minded Australian who wanted to create practical tools families could actually use.

POSH was built from lived experience — not from a government office
Parents Online Safety Hub:
POSH exists to help parents better understand the digital environments children are growing up in and guide them more safely.

Graeme “Jinglez” Ingle

Graeme is an Australian father, qualified carpenter, and builder who believes protecting children begins with empowering parents through knowledge, awareness, and open conversation.

POSH stands for Parents Online Safety Hub — a platform created to help parents understand the online environments their children are growing up in and reduce risk before problems escalate.

POSH was not created by a large institution. It was built by a parent who saw a gap and decided to build something better.

A family-focused life

At the centre of everything Graeme does is family.

He is part of a large blended family surrounded by children of different ages, from toddlers through to older children and teenagers who see both him and his fiancée as parental figures in their lives.

Some of the children are his own, some are step-children, and others have become part of the wider family circle over time.

For privacy and safety reasons, personal details about the children are kept minimal. What matters most is that POSH was built from real experience caring for and guiding children in today’s digital world.

Children deserve safe environments, honest guidance, and strong families supporting them.

A builder by trade

For more than 16 years, Graeme has worked as a carpenter and builder across Victoria.

Builders naturally look for solutions. When something is not working, you do not just complain about it — you design something better and build it.

POSH was created with that same mindset.

The positive side of online communities

The internet is often spoken about only as a dangerous place for children, but it can also create real friendships and positive communities when used responsibly.

Graeme met his fiancée through online gaming while playing Fortnite, and several of his closest friendships began years earlier through Call of Duty online gaming communities.

Those friendships later moved into the real world, including trips and meetups where online connections became genuine face-to-face friendships.

The internet itself is not the enemy. Lack of awareness, structure, and guidance is.

Why POSH was created

Many safety campaigns tell parents to “talk to your kids about online safety” — but very few explain what to say, when to say it, or how those conversations should change as children grow.

That gap is one of the main reasons POSH was created.

  • What to say
  • When to say it
  • How conversations should change with age
  • How to approach harder topics calmly and safely

One of the strongest resources on the platform is the POSH Parent Scripts, built to help parents start age-appropriate conversations more confidently.

Independent and community-driven

POSH is an independent project.

It is not government funded and not backed by large institutions. It has been built from the ground up with the goal of helping families navigate online safety together.

The goal is simple: practical guidance, clearer awareness, and stronger families.

The bigger vision

POSH is only the beginning.

The broader long-term vision includes stronger family supports, community-focused resources, safer environments for parents and children, and practical solutions that help families stay connected.

  • Family play and visitation centres
  • Community support networks for struggling families
  • Innovative housing ideas including container homes
Strong families create stronger communities

Why this matters

POSH was built because too many parents are overwhelmed, too many children are exposed too early, and too many families are left trying to work it all out alone.

If POSH helps one parent ask better questions, tighten safer settings, or have one calmer conversation with their child, it matters.
