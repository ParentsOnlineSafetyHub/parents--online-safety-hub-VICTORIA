Virtual Reality

VR creates immersive voice environments. Children can interact with strangers in real-time spaces that feel far more personal than normal gaming.

Why VR exposure is high

VR environments allow players to speak naturally, move as avatars, and interact in ways that feel closer to real life.

Private rooms, voice chat, and immersive social spaces can build trust very quickly.

VR voice chat environments can escalate contact faster than most games

Why VR can become especially risky for children

  • Immersion makes strangers feel more familiar much faster than text-only chat.
  • Mixed-age users often share the same public spaces.
  • Private worlds reduce visibility and make one-on-one contact easier.
  • Children may be exposed to adult humour, adult behaviour, or sexualised interactions quickly.
  • Contact often moves from VR into Discord or other private apps.
The biggest risk is usually not the headset itself. It is the live social contact happening inside it.

VR safety settings to check

1) Restrict voice chat where possible

2) Disable private room invites from strangers

3) Keep younger users out of public worlds

4) Monitor friend lists and followers

5) Avoid unsupervised VR use for younger children

If a child is using VR regularly, parents should treat it like a live social environment, not just a game device.

What parents should watch for

  • Adults spending unusual amounts of time with children
  • Private room invitations
  • Players encouraging outside contact
  • Requests to move to Discord or other apps
  • Children becoming emotionally attached to someone they only know in VR

How contact often escalates in VR

What starts as casual interaction can become private contact very quickly.

Meet in a public world
↓
Talk regularly through voice chat
↓
Build familiarity and trust
↓
Move into private rooms
↓
Move to Discord or another app
If the contact becomes more private, more emotional, or more secretive, the risk is increasing.
