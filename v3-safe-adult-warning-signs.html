Safe Adult Warning Signs

Children are safer when every trusted adult knows what to notice. This guide helps parents, grandparents, carers, teachers, coaches, babysitters, older siblings, youth workers, and family friends recognise online risk earlier without jumping straight to panic or blame.

Warning signs are patterns, not proof

One sign on its own may not mean danger. A child hiding a phone once, becoming moody one afternoon, or deleting a message does not automatically prove grooming, exploitation, bullying, or blackmail.

The concern grows when several signs appear together, repeat over time, or connect to secrecy, pressure, fear, threats, gifts, sexual requests, private contact, or moving conversations somewhere adults cannot easily see.

POSH rule: warning signs are not a reason to accuse. They are a reason to slow down, stay calm, look closer, and keep the child connected to safe adults.

The warning sign pattern

Online harm often builds in stages. It may begin as friendship, attention, help, gifts, jokes, or gaming contact before turning into secrecy, pressure, fear, or control.

Device and behaviour signs

Devices can become the visible part of a hidden pressure pattern. Adults should not assume every device habit is dangerous, but they should notice when behaviour changes suddenly or becomes secretive.

Hiding the screen

The child quickly turns the screen away, closes apps, locks the phone, switches tabs, or changes behaviour when an adult walks near.

Panic when asked

The child becomes unusually fearful, angry, defensive, tearful, or panicked when asked who they are talking to or what app they are using.

Deleted messages

Messages, call logs, chats, friend requests, usernames, photos, or app histories are repeatedly deleted or hidden.

Hidden accounts or apps

The child has accounts adults do not know about, secret usernames, secondary profiles, hidden folders, vault apps, or apps that look harmless but are used privately.

Late-night use

The child is online late at night, wakes to reply to messages, sleeps with the device hidden, or seems tired from secret online contact.

Fear of losing the device

The child reacts as though losing access would be catastrophic, not simply disappointing. This can mean the device is connected to pressure, dependency, or fear.

Emotional warning signs

Online risk does not always appear as a device problem. Sometimes the first sign is emotional. The child may seem different before adults understand why.

Sudden mood changes

The child becomes anxious, angry, withdrawn, unusually quiet, tearful, reactive, distracted, or emotionally flat after using a device.

One person affects everything

The child’s mood seems controlled by one online friend, gaming contact, group chat, romantic interest, older person, or private account.

Fear and confusion

The child seems scared but cannot explain why, says “it is complicated,” avoids questions, or acts like telling an adult will make things worse.

Shame or self-blame

The child says they are stupid, bad, disgusting, responsible, or that nobody will understand what happened.

Withdrawal from normal life

The child loses interest in family, school, sport, friends, hobbies, sleep, or usual routines because the online world has become too emotionally powerful.

Overreaction to small limits

Normal boundaries around device use create extreme distress because the child may feel pressure to reply, hide something, or keep contact going.

Contact and communication warning signs

Online safety concerns often involve who is contacting the child, where the conversation is happening, and whether the contact is becoming more private.

Unknown online friends

The child talks often to people adults do not know, especially if the person is older, secretive, emotionally intense, or connected through gaming, Discord, Snapchat, TikTok, Instagram, Telegram, or private groups.

Moving to another app

A contact begins in a game or public space, then moves to private messages, Discord, Snapchat, WhatsApp, Telegram, Instagram, or another app that is harder for adults to see.

Private jokes and secret language

The child uses coded language, private references, usernames, nicknames, or inside jokes connected to someone they do not want adults to know about.

Pressure to reply

The child feels they must respond quickly, keep streaks alive, stay in voice chat, answer messages late at night, or avoid making someone angry.

Gift, currency, and reward warning signs

Gifts can look harmless to adults who do not understand games or online platforms. But in some situations, gifts can create pressure, loyalty, secrecy, debt, or dependency.

Free Robux, skins, boosts, or currency

The child receives or is offered game currency, Robux, skins, battle passes, boosts, ranks, rare items, or account help from someone online.

Gifts with conditions

The gift is connected to secrecy, private chats, loyalty, photos, emotional pressure, voice chat, or moving to another app.

Feeling like they owe someone

The child feels they have to keep talking, reply faster, stay private, or do something uncomfortable because someone helped them or gave them something.

Unexplained purchases or subscriptions

There are unexplained charges, subscriptions, in-game purchases, gift cards, account changes, or pressure to spend money.

High-risk warning signs

Some signs should move adults from “watch and ask calmly” into “act now.” These signs do not need perfect proof before adults take safety seriously.

Threats

Someone threatens to expose, embarrass, hurt, report, shame, or punish the child if they tell, block, or stop replying.

Blackmail

Someone demands money, photos, more messages, secrecy, or continued contact to stop them sharing something.

Sexual requests

Someone asks for photos, videos, body images, sexual talk, private calls, livestreams, or sexualised roleplay.

Meeting in person

Someone online asks to meet, offers transport, asks for an address, school, location, routine, or tries to arrange real-world contact.

Fear of consequences

The child believes something terrible will happen if they tell an adult, block the person, leave the group, or stop replying.

Adult involvement

The person appears older, manipulative, controlling, sexually interested, or is trying to isolate the child from safe adults.

If these signs appear, do not shame, do not delete first, and do not confront the person. Preserve evidence where safe and move to urgent help or reporting guidance.

What to say when you notice signs

The safest first words are calm, non-accusing, and open. The goal is to invite the child toward safety, not push them deeper into hiding.

When they hide the device

“I noticed you seemed worried when I came near. I am not here to attack you. I want to understand if something is making you uncomfortable.”

When they seem scared

“You look like something is weighing on you. You will not be in trouble for telling me something that feels wrong.”

When they delete messages

“Deleting messages usually means something feels stressful or private. I need to understand what is happening, and I will stay calm.”

When someone told them not to tell

“Safe people do not ask children to hide unsafe things. You did the right thing by talking to a safe adult.”

What not to do when you notice signs

Adults can accidentally make the situation worse by reacting too strongly, blaming the child, deleting evidence, or confronting the person involved.

Do not accuse first

Accusation can trigger denial, fear, or more hiding.

Do not shame

Shame makes children feel alone and responsible.

Do not grab and explode

Sudden panic can make the child more afraid of adults than the online risk.

Do not delete first

Important evidence may disappear before it can be saved.

Do not contact the suspected person

This can escalate risk, trigger threats, or cause evidence to be deleted.

Do not promise secrecy

Promise support. If safety is involved, the right adults or services may need to help.

When warning signs become urgent

Some warning signs need calm action now, not more waiting.

Move faster if there are threats, blackmail, sexual requests, requests for photos, talk of meeting, pressure to keep secrets, fear, coercion, gifts being used as pressure, or an adult or unknown person trying to isolate the child.

Stay calm. Preserve evidence where safe. Reduce unsafe access. Involve the right adult. Get help.

The safe adult rule

A safe adult does not need to understand every app, every game, every slang word, or every device setting before helping. A safe adult needs to recognise patterns, stay calm, keep the child talking, avoid shame, and know when to involve help.

Shared message: “You can tell me. You are not in trouble for speaking up. We will slow this down together.”
