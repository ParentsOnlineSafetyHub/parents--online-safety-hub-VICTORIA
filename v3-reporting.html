If something feels wrong, act early, stay calm, preserve evidence, and protect your child first.

If you are worried, do not ignore it
STAY CALM. SAVE EVIDENCE. PROTECT THE CHILD.
Many parents panic when they discover something disturbing online. That reaction is understandable, but the most helpful first response is calm action.
Your child needs safety first, not panic first.
What you do in the first few moments can make a big difference to both protection and reporting.

First priority

If a child may be at risk, focus on immediate safety first.

Then preserve evidence before deleting, blocking, or confronting anyone.

What to do immediately

  • Stay calm and keep your child close.
  • Reassure your child they are not in trouble.
  • Stop further contact if safe to do so.
  • Take screenshots of chats, usernames, profiles, friend lists, and messages.
  • Write down dates, times, app names, and what happened.
  • Do not delete evidence unless police specifically advise it later.
  • Do not force your child into a panicked interview while they are distressed.
Children disclose more clearly when they feel safe, believed, and not blamed.

What not to do

  • Do not immediately confront the suspected offender.
  • Do not threaten your child or shame them for what happened.
  • Do not delete accounts, chats, or images before preserving evidence.
  • Do not post accusations publicly without advice.
  • Do not assume blocking alone solves the risk if contact has already occurred.
A rushed reaction can destroy evidence, increase fear, or make the child less likely to keep talking.

Simple reporting flow

If you believe your child has been targeted, this is the safest basic path to follow.

Notice the concern or red flag
↓
Calm the child and stop further contact
↓
Save screenshots and account details
↓
Record what happened in simple notes
↓
Report to the platform and relevant authorities
↓
Get support for the child and family
The clearer your evidence trail is, the easier it is for platforms and authorities to understand what happened.

How to talk to your child

  • Use a calm voice and short questions.
  • Tell them: “You are not in trouble.”
  • Tell them: “I’m glad you told me.”
  • Ask: “Can you show me what happened?”
  • Avoid leading questions that suggest answers.
  • Do not force details if they are frightened or shutting down.
A child who feels believed is more likely to keep talking. A child who feels blamed may shut down.

Report on the platform too

In many cases, you should report the account or content inside the app or platform as well.

  • Use the in-app report feature.
  • Block the account after preserving evidence if needed.
  • Record the report reference number if one is given.
  • Take screenshots of the report submission if possible.
Platform reporting does not replace formal reporting when a child is at risk, but it can help stop further contact quickly.

When the situation is serious

If there are sexual messages, requests for images, threats, blackmail, attempts to meet, or any direct exploitation, treat it as urgent.

Do not assume the child can manage it alone or that it will stop by itself.

Serious sexual contact or coercion should always be treated urgently

After reporting, support still matters

  • Keep reassuring your child they did the right thing.
  • Reduce pressure and avoid repeated blame-based questioning.
  • Monitor device safety and privacy settings.
  • Watch for signs of stress, shame, fear, withdrawal, or sleep changes.
  • Seek additional support if your child is struggling emotionally.
Reporting is important, but emotional recovery and trust-building matter just as much afterwards.

Parent action pathway

Keep it simple and work in order.

Protect the child
↓
Preserve evidence
↓
Report safely
↓
Support the child emotionally
↓
Keep watching for further contact or harm
The goal is not just to stop one message. The goal is to protect the child, preserve the case, and reduce ongoing harm.

Why this page matters

Parents often know something feels wrong before they know exactly what to do next.

This page exists to reduce panic, guide safer action, and help families respond in a way that protects both the child and the evidence.

Child safety improves when parents know the next step clearly.
