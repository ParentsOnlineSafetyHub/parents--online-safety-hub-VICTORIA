Reporting Online Safety Concerns (Australia)

Act early. Stay calm. Report properly.
The right first steps can protect a child and preserve evidence.

If a child is in immediate danger

Call 000 immediately

If there is immediate risk or real-world danger, emergency services always come first.

Child Safety First:
Protect the child → stop contact → preserve evidence → report properly.

When to treat it as serious

  • Sexual messages or sexual content
  • Requests for photos, videos, or live camera
  • Threats, blackmail, or sextortion
  • Adult contacting a child repeatedly
  • Pressure to move to private apps
  • Attempts to meet in person
  • Child sexual abuse material concerns
If it feels serious, act early — don’t wait for perfect proof.

Lane 1 — eSafety Commissioner

Use eSafety for platform-based harm, abuse, and harmful content.

  • Cyberbullying involving a child
  • Image-based abuse
  • Serious online harassment
  • Illegal or harmful online content
Report to eSafety Evidence Guide
Use eSafety when the issue is content, harassment, or platform-based harm.

Lane 2 — ACCCE (AFP)

Use ACCCE for grooming, exploitation, and serious child safety risks.

  • Online grooming
  • Sexual conversations with a child
  • Requests to meet a child
  • Child abuse material
  • Sextortion or blackmail
Report to ACCCE ThinkUKnow
If sexual risk or grooming is involved, this is a major reporting path.

Lane 3 — Police

Use police when risk may move into the real world or harm is immediate.

  • You know who the person is
  • Threats or coercion are happening
  • Offline contact may occur
  • The child may be at local risk
If online risk could become real-world danger, include police early.

Evidence collection

Save information before confronting anyone or deleting anything.

  • Screenshots of chats
  • Usernames and profiles
  • Dates and times
  • Links, servers, or group names
  • Messages or files

Do not confront the person before preserving evidence.

How to respond to your child

  • Tell them they are not in trouble
  • Stay calm
  • Ask simple questions
  • Let them show you what happened
  • Do not blame or shame
If a child feels safe, they will keep talking.

What parents should remember

Most serious situations start small and escalate over time.

Acting early, saving evidence, and choosing the right reporting path can change the outcome.
