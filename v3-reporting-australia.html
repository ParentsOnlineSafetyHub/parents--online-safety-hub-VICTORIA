Reporting Online Safety Concerns (Australia)

Act early. Stay calm. Report properly. The right first steps can protect a child and preserve the evidence.

If a child is in immediate danger

Call 000 immediately

If a child is in immediate risk of harm, exploitation, or an unsafe in-person situation, contact emergency services first. Evidence can be gathered afterwards.

Child Safety First:
If something serious has already happened, focus first on protecting the child, stopping further contact, and preserving evidence calmly.

When to treat it seriously

  • Sexual messages or sexualised contact with a child
  • Requests for images, videos, or live video
  • Threats, blackmail, or sextortion
  • Repeated private contact from an adult
  • Pressure to move into private apps or secret conversations
  • Attempts to meet in person
  • Child exploitation material or suspected grooming behaviour
If it feels serious, do not wait for perfect proof before taking protective action.

eSafety Commissioner

Australia’s national online safety regulator.

Use eSafety to report:

  • Cyberbullying involving children
  • Image-based abuse
  • Serious online harassment
  • Illegal or harmful online content
Report to eSafety
eSafety is often the right place for content-based harm, abuse, and platform-related reporting pathways.

ACCCE – Australian Federal Police

The Australian Centre to Counter Child Exploitation (ACCCE) supports responses to online child sexual exploitation concerns.

Use ACCCE for concerns such as:

  • Online grooming
  • Child exploitation material
  • Suspicious sexualised contact with children
  • Predatory behaviour or exploitation risk online
Visit ACCCE ThinkUKnow
ACCCE and AFP-linked resources can help families better understand child exploitation pathways and reporting options.

State Police

You can also report directly to your local police station.

This may be especially important when:

  • You know the person involved
  • There are threats, coercion, or blackmail
  • Offline contact may happen
  • You believe a child is already being targeted or approached locally
If online risk may turn into real-world contact, local police should be treated as part of the response pathway.

What to do first

If something suspicious happens online, work in order.

Protect the child
↓
Stop further contact if safe to do so
↓
Preserve evidence
↓
Report to the right place
↓
Keep supporting the child afterwards

Evidence collection

If something suspicious happens online, preserve as much of the following as you safely can:

  • Take screenshots
  • Record usernames
  • Note dates and times
  • Save profile links, server names, or group names
  • Keep chat logs, messages, and relevant images if safe to do so

Do not confront the individual directly. Preserve the evidence and report through the correct channels.

Even small details like usernames, timestamps, and profile images can matter later.

How to respond to your child

  • Tell them they are not in trouble
  • Use a calm voice and simple questions
  • Let them show you what happened if they can
  • Do not shame them for replying or trusting someone
  • Focus first on safety, not punishment
A child who feels believed is more likely to keep talking. A child who feels blamed may shut down.

What parents should remember

Most cases begin with small conversations that escalate slowly over time.

Acting early can prevent further harm.
