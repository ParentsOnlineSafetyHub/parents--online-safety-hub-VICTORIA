Reporting Online Safety Concerns (Australia)

Act early. Stay calm. Report properly. The right first steps can protect a child and preserve the evidence.

If a child is in immediate danger

Call 000 immediately

If a child is in immediate danger or at risk of harm in Australia, emergency services come first. eSafety and ACCCE both direct urgent danger matters to emergency police response first. 1

Child Safety First:
If something serious may already have happened, focus first on protecting the child, stopping further contact where safe, and preserving evidence calmly. ACCCE’s current guidance also says to collect evidence where it is safe to do so. 2

When to treat it seriously

  • Sexual messages or sexualised contact with a child
  • Requests for images, videos, or live video
  • Threats, blackmail, or sextortion
  • Repeated private contact from an adult
  • Pressure to move into private apps or secret conversations
  • Attempts to meet in person
  • Child sexual abuse material or suspected grooming behaviour
If it feels serious, do not wait for perfect proof before taking protective action. eSafety specifically directs urgent help for child sexual abuse, grooming, and sexual extortion to ACCCE. 3

Lane 1 — eSafety Commissioner

eSafety is Australia’s national online safety regulator. Its report page says it can investigate cyberbullying of children, adult cyber abuse, image-based abuse, and illegal and restricted online content. 4

Use eSafety when the problem fits those reporting categories, especially where the harm is platform-based, content-based, or image-based. Some eSafety matters also require reporting to the platform first, depending on the complaint type. 5

  • Cyberbullying involving a child
  • Image-based abuse
  • Serious online harassment or abuse that fits eSafety’s schemes
  • Illegal or restricted online content
Report to eSafety Evidence & Reporting
eSafety is usually the right Australian lane for reportable online harm categories it regulates. 6

Lane 2 — ACCCE

ACCCE is the Australian Centre to Counter Child Exploitation. It is AFP-led and brings together partners to counter child exploitation. 7

ACCCE’s report page says you can report suspected online grooming or inappropriate contact, conversations where an adult is saying or doing inappropriate things to a child or trying to meet them in person, live streaming and consumption of child sexual abuse material, and coercion or blackmail of children for sexual purposes, including sexual extortion. 8

  • Online grooming
  • Inappropriate sexualised contact with a child
  • Attempts to meet a child in person
  • Child sexual abuse material concerns
  • Sexual extortion involving a child
Report to ACCCE How & What to Report ThinkUKnow
If the concern is grooming, exploitation, sexual extortion, or inappropriate sexual contact involving a child, ACCCE should be treated as a major reporting lane in Australia. 9

Lane 3 — State or local police

Police should be part of the response when the risk may become real-world, when the child is already being threatened or approached, or when immediate harm is possible. ACCCE says immediate danger should go to Triple Zero or local police. 10

  • You know the person involved
  • There are threats, coercion, or blackmail
  • Offline contact may happen
  • You believe a child is already being targeted locally
If online risk may turn into real-world contact, local police should be treated as part of the response pathway. 11

Evidence collection

ACCCE’s current guidance says that if you are concerned a child is at risk, it is important to act and collect evidence where safe to do so. It says this can include screenshots, usernames, platform details, dates, and other information that shows police exactly what happened. 12

  • Take screenshots
  • Record usernames and display names
  • Note dates and times
  • Save profile links, server names, or group names
  • Keep chat logs, messages, and relevant files if safe to do so

Do not confront the individual directly before preserving the evidence.

Evidence & Reporting First 24 Hours
Even small details like usernames, timestamps, and profile information can matter later. 13

How to respond to your child

  • Tell them they are not in trouble
  • Use a calm voice and simple questions
  • Let them show you what happened if they can
  • Do not shame them for replying or trusting someone
  • Focus first on safety, not punishment
A child who feels believed is more likely to keep talking. A child who feels blamed may shut down.

What parents should remember

Most serious situations begin with small contact that escalates over time.

Acting early, preserving evidence, and using the right reporting lane can change the outcome.
