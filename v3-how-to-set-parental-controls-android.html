How To Set Parental Controls on Android

Android gives parents strong tools, but only if they are actually set up properly. The safest device is one where both the system and the apps are controlled.

What parents need to know first

Android safety usually works best through Google Family Link, Play Store controls, app permissions, and device-level rules.

If you only set one piece and ignore the rest, there will usually still be gaps.

A safer Android setup is built in layers, not one setting

Best order to set it up

1) Set up Google Family Link

2) Lock app installs and Play Store content

3) Review app permissions and unknown installs

4) Control screen time and downtime

5) Check privacy, location, and communication exposure

Step 1 — Set up Google Family Link

Google Family Link is one of the main parent tools for Android devices.

Download Google Family Link on the parent device

Link the child device to the parent account properly

Make sure the child is using the supervised account, not a separate hidden Google account

Family Link helps with app approval, screen time, location, and device supervision, but it works best when the device is genuinely under the right Google account.

Step 2 — Restrict Play Store access

Review app, game, and content settings in the Play Store and through Family Link.

  • Require approval for app installs
  • Restrict app age ratings
  • Restrict movies, shows, books, and music where needed
  • Require authentication for purchases

Step 3 — Block risky installs and review permissions

  • Check whether unknown app installs are allowed
  • Review permissions for camera, microphone, contacts, and location
  • Turn off access that apps do not actually need
  • Check whether chat or social apps have broad access to the device
A child may not need to install a “bad” app for risk to rise. Sometimes the problem is what a normal-looking app is allowed to access.

Step 4 — Set screen time and downtime

Use Family Link to set limits around:

  • Daily screen time
  • Bedtime or downtime
  • App-specific time limits
  • Locking the device when needed
Downtime is often just as important as total hours. Late-night isolation increases risk.

Step 5 — Review communication and privacy risk

  • Check messaging apps, DMs, and social apps
  • Review location settings
  • Check browser access and web filtering
  • Review whether apps can connect children to strangers
Device safety is only strong if the main apps are also locked down.

What parents often miss on Android

  • The child has a second Google account
  • Family Link is installed, but supervision is not fully set up
  • Unknown app installs are still allowed
  • The Play Store is restricted, but browsers are wide open
  • The device is safer, but Discord, Snapchat, or Telegram are still risky
