How To Set Parental Controls on Android

Android has strong controls — but only if you actually use them properly. Safety comes from layered setup, not one setting.

Start here:
Lock the biggest access points first — account, app installs, permissions, and communication.

What parents need to understand first

Android safety is built across multiple layers, not one control.

If one layer is left open, children can still access risk through another pathway.

A safer Android setup is built in layers, not one setting

Best setup order

Set up Family Link
↓
Lock app installs & purchases
↓
Restrict permissions & unknown installs
↓
Set screen time & downtime
↓
Check communication apps & privacy
If you skip a step, that gap becomes the easiest entry point.

Step 1 — Set up Google Family Link

This is your main control system for Android.

Install Google Family Link on your device

Link your child’s device to your parent account

Make sure your child is using the supervised account only

If your child can switch accounts, they can bypass your setup.

Step 2 — Lock the Play Store

  • Require approval for all app installs
  • Set age restrictions for apps and games
  • Require password or biometric for purchases
  • Restrict movies, shows, and content where needed
Most risky apps enter through installs. Control this first.

Step 3 — Block unknown installs & review permissions

  • Turn off “install unknown apps”
  • Check camera, microphone, location, and contacts access
  • Remove permissions that apps do not actually need
  • Check chat and social apps for excessive access
Risk is not just what apps are installed — it is what they can access.

Step 4 — Set screen time and downtime

  • Set daily screen limits
  • Set bedtime / downtime
  • Limit specific apps
  • Lock the device when needed
Late-night use + private contact = higher risk.

Step 5 — Check communication and exposure

  • Review messaging apps and DMs
  • Check Discord, Snapchat, Telegram, and similar apps
  • Review browser access and filters
  • Check location sharing settings
The device can be “safe” while the apps are still high-risk.

What parents often miss

  • Child has a second Google account
  • Family Link installed but not fully active
  • Unknown installs still enabled
  • Browser access left wide open
  • High-risk apps still unrestricted
Kids don’t need to hack anything. They just use the gap you missed.

What this setup actually does

  • Slows down unsafe access
  • Reduces stranger contact pathways
  • Makes behaviour easier to see earlier
  • Gives parents time to act before escalation
Controls don’t remove risk — they make it harder, slower, and more visible.

Key takeaway

One setting is not protection.

Layered control is protection.
