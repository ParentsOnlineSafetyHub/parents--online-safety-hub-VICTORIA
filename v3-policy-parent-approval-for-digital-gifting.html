Parent Approval for Digital Gifting

Children should not be able to receive digital gifts from strangers without parental approval. This will not stop every risk, but it can interrupt one of the most common grooming entry points early.

Why this policy matters

Predators often do not begin with obvious threats. They begin with trust-building.

One of the fastest ways to build trust online is through free gifts, game currency, skins, items, boosts, or special help.

Free digital gifts can be used to open the door to grooming
POSH position:
If a child account cannot receive gifts without a parent knowing, one of the most common grooming pathways becomes harder immediately.

The pattern this policy interrupts

Free item / Robux / skin / currency
↓
Child feels lucky or chosen
↓
Gratitude or obligation forms
↓
Private chat opens
↓
Secrecy begins
↓
Manipulation or control grows
This policy interrupts the pattern at the start, not after the harm is already underway.

What this law would require

  • Child accounts cannot receive digital gifts by default without parental approval.
  • Approval must require a parent PIN, password, or verified guardian action.
  • Parents should be able to approve, reject, or block the sender.
  • Gift attempts should be visible to parents on child-linked accounts.
  • Game platforms should make this a child safety default, not an optional hidden setting.
This is not anti-gaming. It is child safety by design.

What counts as digital gifting?

  • Robux, V-Bucks, gems, coins, or game currency
  • Skins, cosmetics, loot, crates, or digital items
  • Boosts, passes, subscriptions, or premium access
  • Tradeable items or account-linked digital rewards
  • Any item used to create gratitude, obligation, or repeated contact

Why this is reasonable

This does not ban games.

This does not ban gifts.

This does not remove normal family use.

It simply means children should not receive outside digital gifts without a parent knowing.

Most parents would assume this protection already exists. In many cases, it does not.

What this would change for families

  • Parents gain visibility earlier
  • Predators lose a fast trust-building tool
  • Children have fewer hidden reward-based contact points
  • Games become safer without banning normal play
  • Risk becomes harder to hide and easier to interrupt

What POSH is saying clearly

If the risk pattern is predictable, safer defaults should not be optional.

This law would not stop all grooming. But it would remove one of the easiest first steps: free digital gifts that build trust, gratitude, and obligation.
