Parent Approval for Digital Gifting

One simple safety change could break a major grooming pattern. Child accounts should not be able to receive digital gifts from others without parent approval.

Why this matters

Online grooming often starts with something that looks harmless: free Robux, skins, gifts, coins, passes, upgrades, or “being generous.”

But that “gift” is often not the end of the interaction. It is the start of trust-building, obligation, and private contact.

Gifting can be the hook that opens the door
POSH position:
Child accounts should not be allowed to receive gifts, currency, items, skins, or paid digital rewards from other users unless a parent approves it with a PIN or password.

The pattern this would disrupt

If gifting needs parent approval, the first hook becomes much harder to use.

What POSH is proposing

  • All child accounts should have gifting turned OFF by default
  • Receiving gifts should require a parent PIN or password
  • Parents should be able to approve trusted senders only
  • Gift history should be visible to parents
  • Games and apps should clearly identify when an item was gifted by another user
  • Child-linked accounts should not be allowed anonymous gifting from strangers

Why this is practical

This is not a radical idea. Games and apps already use parental controls for purchases, downloads, content, and communication.

Extending that same logic to gifting is both practical and overdue.

If platforms can ask for approval before spending money, they can ask for approval before accepting high-risk digital gifts.

Why digital gifts matter more than people think

  • They create fast trust
  • They make children feel noticed or chosen
  • They can create a sense of debt or obligation
  • They make the sender seem kind, helpful, or “safe”
  • They are often used before private contact begins
A digital gift is not always “just a gift.” Sometimes it is the first move in a pattern.

Where this matters most

  • Games with gifting systems
  • Apps with coins, boosts, or paid items
  • Platforms where players can trade items or currency
  • Games where children can receive rewards from other users
  • Any child account linked to social contact with strangers

What parents can do right now

Explain that gifts from strangers are not automatically safe

Ask your child to tell you if anyone offers free items, Robux, skins, or rewards

Set a family rule: no accepting gifts from unknown players without asking first

Check gifting and trading settings where possible

Watch for private chat after “friendly” generosity

What lawmakers and platforms should understand

Child safety does not only depend on reporting after harm. It also depends on removing predictable entry points before harm begins.

Safer defaults matter

Child accounts should be protected automatically

Parental visibility should be built into high-risk features

Design choices can reduce grooming opportunities dramatically

If the pattern is predictable, the platform should not be allowed to ignore it.

What this policy would achieve

  • Reduce gifting as a grooming hook
  • Increase parental visibility
  • Interrupt trust-building from strangers
  • Create safer defaults for minors
  • Push platforms to take child safety more seriously at the design level

This is bigger than one game

This is not just about Roblox, skins, or one platform.

This is about recognising that digital gifting can be used to target children — and building systems that interrupt that pattern early.
