Safe Adult Response System

Children are safer when every trusted adult knows how to respond. This guide gives parents, grandparents, carers, teachers, coaches, babysitters, older siblings, youth workers, and family friends a calm step-by-step system for online safety concerns.

The first response can change everything

When a child hints, admits, or accidentally reveals that something online feels wrong, the adult’s reaction matters. A calm response can keep the child talking. A shocked, angry, or blaming response can push the child back into secrecy.

The goal is not to solve everything in the first minute. The goal is to keep the child safe enough, calm enough, and connected enough to continue telling the truth.

POSH rule: respond first, investigate second. A child who feels safe is more likely to disclose what actually happened.

The POSH safe adult pathway

Every safe adult does not need to be a technology expert. But every safe adult should know this pathway.

A safe adult does not panic, shame, promise secrecy, delete first, or confront the suspected person. A safe adult slows the situation down and keeps the child connected to help.

Step 1: Notice without accusing

Many children do not directly say, “I need help.” They may show discomfort, secrecy, mood changes, fear, withdrawal, or panic around a device.

Notice behaviour

Watch for sudden secrecy, deleted messages, hidden accounts, late-night contact, panic around devices, emotional changes, or a child becoming unusually focused on one online person.

Notice language

Listen for phrases like “you wouldn’t understand,” “I can’t tell you,” “they’ll be angry,” “it’s complicated,” “I’ll get in trouble,” or “please don’t tell anyone.”

Notice pressure

Pressure may involve gifts, secrets, screenshots, threats, emotional dependency, fear of exposure, requests for photos, or being pushed to move to another app.

Notice your own reaction

Adults naturally feel fear, anger, or shock. But the child needs the adult to stay steady first. You can feel strongly without reacting loudly.

Step 2: Stay calm on purpose

Calm does not mean the situation is not serious. Calm means the adult is controlled enough to protect the child properly.

Lower your voice

A quieter voice helps reduce panic. The child may already feel scared, embarrassed, trapped, or ashamed.

Slow your questions

Too many fast questions can feel like interrogation. Ask one question at a time and let the child answer in their own words.

Control your face

Shocked facial reactions can accidentally tell the child, “This is too big to say out loud.”

Helpful line: “I might feel worried because I care about you, but I am going to stay calm and help you through this.”

Step 3: Reassure before investigating

Children often hide because they fear punishment, shame, losing the device, being blamed, or making the situation worse. Reassurance helps them keep talking.

If the child is scared

“You are not in trouble for telling me. I am glad you said something. We can slow this down together.”

If the child feels embarrassed

“You do not have to explain it perfectly. Start with what you can. I will listen first.”

If the child fears losing the device

“The device is not the main issue right now. Your safety matters more. I need to understand what happened.”

If someone told them to keep a secret

“Safe adults do not ask children to hide unsafe things. You did the right thing by telling someone.”

Step 4: Ask calm clarifying questions

The adult needs enough information to understand risk, but the child should not feel cross-examined. Ask practical questions in a calm order.

Who is involved?

“Who is the person or group? Do you know them in real life, from school, through a game, or only online?”

Where did it happen?

“Was it on a game, app, private message, voice chat, livestream, group chat, Discord, Snapchat, Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, or somewhere else?”

What are they asking for?

“Are they asking for secrecy, photos, money, more messages, voice calls, video calls, your location, or to meet?”

Are there threats?

“Has anyone threatened to share something, embarrass you, hurt you, report you, expose you, or make something bad happen?”

Has anything been sent?

“Have any photos, videos, screenshots, personal details, school details, addresses, usernames, or account information been shared?”

Is the child still in contact?

“Are they still messaging you? Are they expecting you to reply soon? Are you worried what happens if you do not reply?”

Step 5: Work out the risk level

Not every concern needs the same response. Use the level of risk to decide what should happen next.

Lower concern

Examples include unsuitable content, too much screen time, annoying messages, minor friendship conflict, or unclear discomfort without threats or sexual pressure.

Response: calm conversation, device visibility, house rules, settings check, and follow-up.

Rising concern

Examples include secrecy, deleted messages, hidden accounts, unknown online friends, pressure to move apps, gifts, late-night contact, or emotional dependency.

Response: check device calmly, reduce private access, involve parent or guardian, preserve key details, and monitor closely.

High concern

Examples include threats, blackmail, sexual requests, photos, fear, coercion, adult involvement, meeting plans, or pressure to keep unsafe secrets.

Response: urgent help, preserve evidence, do not delete first, do not confront the person, and report through the right pathway.

Step 6: Preserve evidence where safe

In serious situations, evidence may matter. Deleting, blocking, confronting, or warning the suspected person too early can make information disappear.

Save the basics

Keep usernames, display names, profile links, messages, screenshots, dates, times, app names, game names, group names, server names, and any contact details.

Do not edit screenshots

Keep screenshots clear and complete where possible. Avoid cropping out usernames, timestamps, or conversation context.

Record the platform

Write down where the contact happened: Roblox, Discord, Snapchat, TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Telegram, WhatsApp, gaming voice chat, or another service.

Do not investigate beyond your role

Safe adults support and escalate. They should not pretend to be the child, bait the person, threaten the person, or run their own investigation.

Step 7: Involve the right adult

A safe adult may not always be the legal decision-maker. Teachers, coaches, babysitters, family friends, older siblings, and grandparents may need to involve the parent, guardian, school safeguarding pathway, organisation lead, or emergency service depending on risk.

If you are the parent or guardian

Take the lead calmly. Support the child, preserve evidence, reduce unsafe contact, review devices, and report if serious risk is involved.

If you are a grandparent or family member

Support the child and involve the parent or guardian unless doing so would place the child at further risk. Keep your language calm and practical.

If you are a teacher or school staff member

Follow school safeguarding and mandatory reporting procedures. Do not promise secrecy. Record concerns factually and escalate through the correct pathway.

If you are a coach, activity leader, or youth worker

Follow your organisation’s child safety policy. Support the child, document what was disclosed, and escalate to the responsible safeguarding person.

If you are an older sibling

You are not responsible for fixing it alone. If a younger child is scared, threatened, pressured, or unsafe, tell a safe adult.

If you are a babysitter or sleepover adult

Keep the child safe in the moment, avoid panic, preserve obvious details, and contact the parent or guardian as soon as appropriate.

What if the child says “please don’t tell”?

This is a common moment. The child may fear punishment, embarrassment, losing their device, getting someone else in trouble, or making threats worse.

Do not promise secrecy if safety may be involved. A safer promise is: “I will not shame you. I will not tell people who do not need to know. But if you might be unsafe, I may need to involve the right adult so we can protect you.”

Promise support, not secrecy.

Safe wording

“I understand why you want this kept quiet. I will be careful with who needs to know. But I cannot keep unsafe things secret.”

Unsafe wording

“I promise I won’t tell anyone,” if the child may be facing threats, sexual contact, blackmail, coercion, or real-world danger.

What not to do

These reactions can accidentally increase secrecy, destroy evidence, or escalate the risk.

Do not shame

Shame makes children feel responsible, dirty, stupid, or trapped.

Do not explode

Panic can teach the child that telling an adult makes everything worse.

Do not delete first

Messages, usernames, timestamps, links, and account details may matter later.

Do not confront the person

This can trigger deletion, threats, escalation, or further pressure on the child.

Do not blame the child

Children can be manipulated through attention, gifts, fear, secrecy, and emotional pressure.

Do not investigate beyond your role

Support, preserve, and escalate. Do not run your own undercover investigation.

When to move faster

Some situations need calm action now. Do not wait for perfect proof if the child may be unsafe.

Move faster if there are threats, blackmail, sexual requests, requests for photos, pressure to keep secrets, talk of meeting in person, fear, coercion, gifts being used as pressure, or an adult or unknown person trying to isolate the child.

Stay calm. Preserve evidence. Reduce unsafe contact. Involve the right adult. Get help.

The safe adult promise

A safe adult cannot promise that nothing will change. Sometimes safety requires action. But a safe adult can promise calm, respect, support, and no shame.

Shared message: “You are not alone. You are not in trouble for telling me. We will slow this down and get the right help.”
