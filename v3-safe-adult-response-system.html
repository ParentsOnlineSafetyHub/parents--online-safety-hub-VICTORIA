Shared Safety Language

Safe Adult Response System

Children are safer when every trusted adult knows how to respond. This page gives parents, grandparents, carers, teachers, coaches, babysitters, older siblings, youth workers, and family friends one calm pathway to follow when something online feels wrong.

The first adult response can change everything

When a child opens up about something online, they may already feel scared, embarrassed, confused, guilty, or worried they will lose their device. If the first adult response is panic, anger, shame, or blame, the child may stop talking.

The safe adult’s job is not to get every detail immediately. The first job is to keep the child safe enough to keep talking.

POSH rule: calm is not weakness. Calm is control. Calm keeps the child connected to protection.

The POSH safe adult pathway

This pathway gives every trusted adult the same order of response. It works whether the adult is a parent, grandparent, teacher, coach, carer, babysitter, or family friend.

2. Reassure the child
↓
3. Keep them talking
↓
4. Do not shame

The system is simple on purpose. In stressful moments, adults need a clear order — not a complicated lecture.

Step 1: Stay calm

A child watches the adult’s face, tone, and body language before deciding how much more to say. If the adult explodes, the child may protect the secret instead of asking for help.

What calm sounds like

“I am glad you told me. You are not in trouble for speaking up. We are going to slow this down together.”

What panic can do

Panic can make the child feel blamed, unsafe, embarrassed, or responsible for the adult’s emotions.

Calm does not mean passive

You can be calm and still act quickly. Calm means you are thinking clearly and protecting the child, not ignoring the concern.

Avoid the big reaction first

Avoid yelling, grabbing the device, calling the child names, blaming them, or demanding every detail at once.

Step 2: Reassure the child

Children often fear the adult response more than the online problem. They may worry they will lose their phone, lose friends, get in trouble, be judged, or make the situation worse.

If they are scared

“You do not have to handle this alone. I am going to stay with you and help you.”

If they feel embarrassed

“You do not have to explain it perfectly. Start with what you can. I will listen.”

If they think it is their fault

“Someone pressuring you, threatening you, or asking you to hide things is not your fault.”

If they fear losing the device

“The device is not the main issue right now. Your safety is the main issue.”

Step 3: Keep them talking

The adult does not need to ask perfect questions. The safest questions are calm, open, and simple. They help the child give enough information to understand the level of risk.

Start broad

“Can you tell me what happened from the beginning, as best as you can?”

Ask about pressure

“Did anyone ask you to keep this secret, delete messages, send photos, or move to another app?”

Ask about fear

“Are you worried they will do something if you block them or tell an adult?”

Ask about contact

“What app, game, chat, account, username, or group was this happening through?”

Ask enough to understand safety. Do not turn the child’s first disclosure into a full interrogation.

Step 4: Do not shame

Shame is one of the strongest reasons children hide online problems. A child may already feel responsible, tricked, embarrassed, or afraid. Shame makes them less likely to ask for help next time.

Do not call them stupid

Children are still learning risk, pressure, boundaries, trust, and online manipulation.

Do not blame curiosity

Curiosity, loneliness, friendship, attention, and wanting to belong can all be used against children.

Do not make it about punishment first

If the first message is “you are losing everything,” the child may hide more next time.

Correction can come later. Safety comes first. Connection comes first. Understanding comes first.

Step 5: Do not delete first

Evidence may matter if there are threats, blackmail, sexual requests, grooming, harassment, or exploitation. Adults often want to delete everything immediately because it feels safer, but deleting first can make reporting harder.

What may matter

Usernames, profile links, messages, screenshots, account handles, group names, server names, timestamps, phone numbers, payment requests, threats, and platform names.

Do not spread sensitive material

If intimate or illegal material is involved, do not forward it around. Preserve safely and follow proper reporting guidance.

Pause before blocking

Blocking may be necessary, but first consider whether evidence needs to be captured and whether the child is in immediate danger.

Do not confront the person

Threatening, baiting, or messaging the suspected person can escalate risk, trigger deletion, or make the situation harder to report.

Step 6: Involve the right adult

Different adults have different roles. A grandparent, babysitter, older sibling, teacher, coach, youth worker, and parent do not all have the same responsibility. But they can all help connect the child to the right protection.

Parents and guardians

Usually the main adult who needs to know, support the child, check devices, make safety decisions, and report if needed.

Schools and organisations

Teachers, coaches, youth workers, and activity leaders may need to follow safeguarding, child safety, incident, or reporting pathways.

Emergency or reporting help

Serious risk may need urgent help, platform reporting, police, eSafety, child protection, or other proper services depending on the situation.

Do not promise secrecy if safety is involved. Promise calm support instead.

Step 7: Act early if risk is serious

Some online situations need faster action. Waiting for perfect proof can leave a child more exposed.

Move faster if there are threats, blackmail, sexual requests, requests for photos, pressure to keep secrets, talk of meeting in person, coercion, gifts being used as pressure, a child being frightened, or an unknown person trying to move the child somewhere more private.

Stay calm. Support the child. Preserve evidence where safe. Reduce unsafe contact. Get help.

What every safe adult should say

Children need clear words from adults. These phrases help reduce fear, shame, and secrecy.

“You are not in trouble for telling me.”

This separates disclosure from punishment and helps the child keep talking.

“I am glad you said something.”

This shows the child that speaking up was the right choice.

“We can slow this down.”

This helps the child feel less trapped by pressure, threats, secrecy, or urgency.

“I cannot promise secrecy, but I can promise support.”

This is especially important for teachers, coaches, carers, youth workers, and other adults with safeguarding responsibilities.

What every safe adult should avoid

Unsafe reactions can accidentally push children deeper into hiding. Adults do not need to be perfect, but they need to avoid the responses that make children feel blamed or alone.

Do not shame

Avoid labels, insults, disgust, mocking, or blaming language.

Do not interrogate

Ask enough to understand safety. Do not force every detail at once.

Do not promise secrecy

If safety is involved, the right adults or services may need to know.

Do not delete first

Preserve important information before removing access where safe.

Do not confront the person

This can escalate risk or cause evidence to disappear.

Do not minimise

If a child feels unsafe, take it seriously even if the full picture is not clear yet.

Shared safety language for families and communities

The goal is not to turn every adult into a digital investigator. The goal is to create a safer circle around the child. When every trusted adult understands the same warning signs and uses the same calm language, children are less likely to feel trapped between secrecy and punishment.

Shared message: “You can tell us. You are not alone. We will stay calm. We will help.”
