For Every Safe Adult

Safe Adult Response System

When a child hints that something online feels wrong, the adult response can either keep the child talking or push them deeper into secrecy. This system gives every trusted adult a calm pathway to follow before panic takes over.

The first response matters

Children often test the adult’s reaction before they tell the full story. They may start with a small sentence, a half-truth, a nervous question, or a vague comment about someone online. If the adult reacts with anger, shame, panic, or punishment, the child may stop talking.

The goal is not to get every detail immediately. The goal is to make the child safe enough to keep explaining.

POSH rule: stay calm first, ask better questions second, take protective action third.

The POSH safe adult response pathway

Every adult does not need to be a technology expert. But every safe adult should know the response order.

A calm adult is not a passive adult. Calm means controlled, protective, and less likely to make the child shut down.

Step 1: Stay calm

The child may already be scared, ashamed, embarrassed, confused, or worried about losing devices. If the adult becomes explosive, the child may decide that telling the truth makes everything worse.

What calm sounds like

“Thank you for telling me. You are not in trouble for speaking up. I am going to stay calm and help you work out what to do next.”

What panic can sound like

“How could you do that? Give me your phone. I told you not to talk to strangers. You are never using this again.”

Even if the adult feels angry inside, the child needs to see control on the outside.

Step 2: Reassure the child

Reassurance does not mean ignoring risk. It means making sure the child understands they are not alone, they are not being blamed for asking for help, and the adult is focused on safety.

If the child is scared

“You do not have to handle this by yourself. We can slow it down together.”

If the child feels ashamed

“You are not bad for telling me. If someone pressured you, tricked you, or scared you, the safest thing is to talk about it.”

If the child fears punishment

“I am not here to explode at you. I need to understand what happened so we can keep you safe.”

If someone told them to stay quiet

“Safe adults do not ask children to hide unsafe things. You did the right thing telling someone.”

Step 3: Listen before acting

Adults often want to grab the phone, demand names, message the person, delete accounts, or call everyone immediately. Sometimes urgent action is needed, but first the adult should understand enough to avoid making the situation worse.

Ask what happened

“Can you tell me what started this? Was it in a game, app, group chat, livestream, message, or somewhere else?”

Ask about pressure

“Is anyone making you feel like you have to reply, send something, hide something, or keep talking?”

Ask about fear

“What are you most worried will happen if an adult helps?”

Ask about evidence

“Are there messages, usernames, screenshots, links, accounts, photos, or chats that show what happened?”

Ask about secrecy

“Has anyone told you not to tell adults, delete messages, use another app, or keep this private?”

Ask about urgency

“Has anyone threatened you, asked for photos, talked about meeting, or said they will share something?”

Step 4: Preserve evidence before deleting

In serious situations, evidence can matter. Screenshots, usernames, profile links, chat history, timestamps, voice notes, platform names, server names, and account handles may help parents, schools, platforms, or authorities understand what happened.

Useful evidence can include

  • usernames, display names, account handles, profile links, and IDs
  • screenshots of messages, threats, requests, gifts, links, or pressure
  • dates, times, apps, games, servers, groups, or chat names
  • photos of profiles, friend lists, message threads, or payment/gifting records
  • notes about what the child said and when they said it

Do not rush to delete everything first

Deleting messages, blocking accounts, leaving groups, or confronting someone may remove evidence or cause the person to escalate, threaten, or disappear.

If the child is in immediate danger, safety comes first. Otherwise, preserve what you can calmly before changing access.

Step 5: Involve the right adult

Not every adult has the same responsibility. A parent may act directly. A teacher may need to follow school safeguarding procedures. A coach may need to notify a club safety officer. A grandparent may need to tell the parent. An older sibling may need to find a safe adult quickly.

Parents and guardians

Usually lead the home response: supporting the child, checking devices, preserving evidence, reducing contact, and seeking help if needed.

Grandparents

Should stay calm, listen, avoid shame, and involve the parent or guardian unless doing so would place the child at further risk.

Teachers and school staff

Should follow school policy and safeguarding pathways. Do not promise secrecy. Record concerns carefully and escalate through the correct process.

Coaches and activity leaders

Should support the child calmly and follow club, organisation, or child safety procedures. Avoid investigating beyond the role.

Older siblings

Should not be made responsible for fixing adult-level safety problems. Their job is to tell a safe adult if something unsafe is happening.

Babysitters and family friends

Should not ignore concerning behaviour. Record what happened, keep the child safe, and notify the responsible adult as soon as appropriate.

Step 6: Reduce unsafe access

Once the adult has listened and preserved what is needed, the next step is reducing risk. This does not always mean removing every device forever. It means reducing the pathways that allowed pressure, secrecy, or unsafe contact.

Reduce private contact

Review friend lists, private messages, group chats, servers, followers, voice chat, livestreams, and apps where contact can happen away from adult visibility.

Move devices into visible spaces

Phones, tablets, laptops, and gaming devices should be used in more visible family areas while the concern is being understood.

Check account settings

Review privacy, direct messages, friend requests, location sharing, profile visibility, app installs, downloads, payment settings, and parental controls.

Change the routine

Reduce late-night access, bedroom device use, unsupervised voice chat, unknown friend requests, and one-on-one private communication with people the family cannot verify.

Step 7: Escalate if urgent

Some situations need faster action. Do not wait for a perfect explanation if the child may be in danger.

Move faster if there are threats, blackmail, sexual requests, requests for photos, sexual images, talk of meeting in person, coercion, fear, pressure to keep secrets, an unknown adult, or someone using screenshots, gifts, money, or shame to control the child.

If there is immediate danger, contact emergency services. If online exploitation, blackmail, or child sexual abuse material may be involved, use the correct reporting pathway.

What safe adults should avoid

These responses can accidentally increase secrecy, destroy evidence, or make the child less likely to ask for help again.

Do not shame

Avoid words like stupid, dirty, dramatic, careless, disgusting, or “you should have known better.”

Do not interrogate

Too many fast questions can overwhelm the child. Ask slowly, listen, and let them explain in pieces.

Do not promise secrecy

Say, “I will support you calmly,” not “I will never tell anyone.”

Do not contact the suspected person

Confronting someone online can trigger deletion, threats, escalation, or more pressure on the child.

Do not delete first

Preserve what you can unless immediate safety requires another action.

Do not blame the device alone

Devices matter, but the deeper issue may be pressure, secrecy, manipulation, dependency, or fear.

Safe adult scripts

These lines help adults stay steady when they do not know what to say.

If the child hints at danger

“I can see this is hard to say. You are not in trouble for telling me. Start wherever you can.”

If the child says they promised not to tell

“Unsafe secrets are different from privacy. If someone has made you scared to tell, that is exactly when a safe adult should help.”

If the child fears losing access

“We may need to make some safety changes, but I am not here to punish you for being honest.”

If the child feels responsible

“If someone pressured, tricked, threatened, or manipulated you, the responsibility is not all on you. We are going to deal with the safety problem.”

If the child is scared of screenshots

“We will not rush. We will save what we need, get help if needed, and make a plan.”

If the child shuts down

“You do not have to explain everything right now. I am here. We can come back to it, but I need to make sure you are safe.”

Response by role

The same response system applies to every adult, but the action pathway changes depending on the adult’s role.

Grandparents

Listen calmly, avoid judgement, write down key details, avoid deleting evidence, and involve the parent or guardian unless doing so would increase risk.

Grandparents Guide

Parents and step-parents

Lead the home response: support the child, preserve evidence, check devices, reduce private contact, update rules, and report if needed.

Carers and guardians

Teachers and school staff

Teacher Training

Coaches and activity leaders

Keep the child safe in the moment, do not handle it privately alone, and escalate through club, organisation, or child safety procedures.

Older siblings

Older Sibling Guide

After the first response

The first conversation is only the beginning. A good response continues after the immediate concern is handled.

Rebuild trust

The child needs to know that telling the truth led to support, not humiliation. This makes future disclosure more likely.

Reset device rules

Review bedrooms, late-night access, private chats, friend requests, app downloads, accounts, and visibility.

Teach the pattern

Help the child understand pressure, secrecy, gifts, off-platform movement, blackmail, and unsafe attention.

Keep checking in

Children may reveal more over time. Use calm follow-up conversations rather than one huge interrogation.

The shared adult message

Children are safer when the adults around them use the same calm language. The child should not hear one adult shame them, one adult ignore them, one adult panic, and one adult blame the device. The message should be steady.

Shared adult message: “You can tell us. You are not alone. We will stay calm. We will help.”
