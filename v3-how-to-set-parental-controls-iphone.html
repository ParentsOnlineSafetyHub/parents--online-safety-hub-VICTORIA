How To Set Parental Controls on iPhone

iPhones are not safe by default. You need to lock the right settings properly if you want the device to support your parenting instead of working against it.

Start here:
Lock the main access points first — Screen Time, passcode, app installs, content, and communication settings.

What parents need to know first

Apple gives parents useful tools through Screen Time, content restrictions, communication safety, and purchase controls.

But if you do not turn them on and protect them with a parent-only passcode, they do not stop much on their own.

A device is only as safe as the settings you actually lock in

Best setup order

Turn on Screen Time
↓
Create a parent-only passcode
↓
Lock app installs, deletions, and purchases
↓
Restrict content and websites
↓
Check communication and privacy exposure
If one layer stays open, that gap becomes the easiest way around your setup.

Step 1 — Turn on Screen Time

On the iPhone, go to:

Settings → Screen Time

Turn it on, then choose the child option if you are setting it up for your child’s phone.

Screen Time is the main control system. If it is not switched on properly, the rest of the setup is much weaker.

Step 2 — Set a parent-only Screen Time passcode

This is the part many parents miss.

Set a Screen Time passcode the child does not know

Do not reuse a code they can easily guess

Keep the passcode parent-controlled only

If the child knows the passcode, they can often undo the safety setup without much trouble.

Step 3 — Lock purchases and installs

Go to:

Settings → Screen Time → Content & Privacy Restrictions → iTunes & App Store Purchases

  • Set Installing Apps to Don’t Allow if needed
  • Set Deleting Apps to Don’t Allow if needed
  • Set In-App Purchases to Don’t Allow if needed
  • Require password for purchases
Most risky apps enter through installs. Control this access point early.

Step 4 — Restrict content

Go to:

Settings → Screen Time → Content & Privacy Restrictions → Content Restrictions

  • Restrict explicit music, movies, and shows
  • Restrict app age ratings
  • Restrict websites and adult content
  • Review web search and Siri content settings
Content settings matter because risk does not only come from strangers. It can also come from what the device is allowed to serve up.

Step 5 — Set communication limits

Review who your child can communicate with during allowed time and downtime.

Settings → Screen Time → Communication Limits

This helps reduce contact exposure, especially late at night or when the child is more isolated.

Private contact risk often rises when communication is open but supervision is low.

Step 6 — Review privacy and location settings

  • Check Location Services
  • Review app permissions for camera, microphone, photos, and contacts
  • Turn off unnecessary tracking and access
  • Review whether apps can share location or contacts
Many risks come from what apps are allowed to access, not just what the child taps.

What parents often miss on iPhone

  • The child knows or guesses the Screen Time passcode
  • Safari still gives access to sites they thought were blocked
  • Messaging and private contact risks are still open inside apps
  • Family Sharing is not set up properly
  • The device is locked down, but the apps are not
A safer phone setup still needs safer app settings, house rules, and calm parent visibility.

What this setup actually does

  • Reduces risky installs and spending
  • Limits access to explicit or mature content
  • Makes communication exposure easier to control
  • Helps parents spot problems earlier instead of later
Settings do not remove all risk. They make risk harder, slower, and easier to notice.

Device settings are strongest when they are backed by clear rules, regular check-ins, and calm conversations.

Key takeaway

One setting is not protection.

Layered control is protection.

A safer setup today can prevent bigger problems later
