How To Set Parental Controls on iPhone

iPhones are not safe by default. You need to set the right controls properly if you want the device to support your parenting instead of working against it.

What parents need to know first

Apple gives parents useful tools through Screen Time, content restrictions, communication safety, and purchase controls.

But if you do not turn them on and lock them with a parent-only passcode, they do not protect much on their own.

A device is only as safe as the settings you actually lock in

Best order to set it up

1) Turn on Screen Time

2) Create a parent-only Screen Time passcode

3) Block app installs, deletions, and in-app purchases

4) Restrict explicit content and websites

5) Review communication and privacy settings

Step 1 — Turn on Screen Time

On the iPhone, go to:

Settings → Screen Time

Turn it on, then choose the child option if you are setting it up for your child’s phone.

The Screen Time passcode should be parent-controlled, not something the child can guess.

Step 2 — Lock down purchases and installs

Go to:

Settings → Screen Time → Content & Privacy Restrictions → iTunes & App Store Purchases

  • Set Installing Apps to Don’t Allow if needed
  • Set Deleting Apps to Don’t Allow if needed
  • Set In-App Purchases to Don’t Allow if needed
  • Require password for purchases

Step 3 — Restrict content

Go to:

Settings → Screen Time → Content & Privacy Restrictions → Content Restrictions

  • Restrict explicit music, movies, and shows
  • Restrict app age ratings
  • Restrict websites and adult content
  • Review web search and Siri content settings
Content settings matter because risk does not only come from strangers. It can also come from what the device is allowed to serve up.

Step 4 — Set communication limits

Review who your child can communicate with during allowed time and downtime.

Settings → Screen Time → Communication Limits

This helps reduce contact exposure, especially at night or when the child is more isolated.

Step 5 — Review privacy and location settings

  • Check Location Services
  • Review app permissions for camera, microphone, photos, and contacts
  • Turn off unnecessary tracking and access
  • Review whether apps can share location or contacts
Many risks come from what apps are allowed to access, not just what the child taps.

What parents often miss on iPhone

  • The child knows or guesses the Screen Time passcode
  • Safari still gives access to sites they thought were blocked
  • Messaging and private contact risks are still open inside apps
  • Family Sharing is not set up properly
  • The device is locked down, but the apps are not
