How To Set Parental Controls on a Tablet

Tablets often look safer than they really are.
If the setup is loose, a tablet can become the easiest hidden device in the house.

Start here:
Whether it is an iPad or an Android tablet, the goal is the same:
lock the device, limit the apps, reduce private contact, and keep visibility high.

Why tablet setup matters

Many parents treat tablets like lower-risk entertainment devices.

But tablets can still run browsers, messaging apps, games, social apps, video apps, and account-based platforms.

A tablet is still a communication device if the settings allow it

Step 1 — Know which tablet you are setting up

iPad uses Apple Screen Time and Apple restrictions

Android tablets usually rely on Family Link, device settings, and Google Play controls

iPad Controls Android Controls All Device Controls

Step 2 — Lock the basics first

  • Set a parent-controlled passcode or PIN
  • Restrict app installs where possible
  • Restrict deleting apps where possible
  • Restrict in-app purchases
  • Lock age-based content settings
A tablet becomes much safer when it cannot quietly change shape after setup.

Step 3 — Check the real risk layers

The main question is not just what games are on the tablet. It is what pathways the tablet gives your child to other people and platforms.

Messaging apps

Game chats

Voice chat

Web browsers

YouTube or video apps

Social apps and hidden second accounts

The biggest gaps on tablets are often browsers, video platforms, and apps parents assumed were harmless.

Step 4 — Set time limits and usage boundaries

  • Set app limits for high-use categories
  • Set downtime for sleep and school hours
  • Keep tablet use visible where possible
  • Do not let the tablet become the “easy private screen” in the home
A tablet is safer when it stays visible, predictable, and tied to clear rules.

Step 5 — Match the tablet to your house rules

No new apps without parent awareness

No moving into chat apps secretly

No staying up late on a tablet behind closed doors

No deleting apps or accounts to hide activity

Controls are much stronger when the family standard backs them up clearly.

What parents often get wrong

Thinking tablets are lower risk than phones by default

Focusing only on screen time

Ignoring browsers and chat pathways

Leaving app downloads too open

Tablets are often treated too softly, and that creates blind spots

A tablet becomes safer when it has fewer hidden pathways, stronger controls, and better parent visibility.

Safe is not the device type. Safe is the setup around it
