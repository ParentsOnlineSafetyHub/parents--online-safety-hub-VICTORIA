Safer Defaults for Child Accounts

Child safety should not depend on parents finding every setting manually. Child accounts should start safer by default.

Why this matters

Most parents do not know every setting on every app, game, console, or platform their child uses.

That means children are often exposed to strangers, private messages, gifting systems, location sharing, and risky features simply because the default settings were never designed with child safety first.

If safety is optional, risk becomes normal
POSH position:
Child accounts should have the safest reasonable settings turned on automatically, with parents choosing to loosen them later if they want to.

What safer defaults would look like

  • Direct messages OFF by default for child accounts
  • Stranger contact restricted by default
  • Location sharing OFF by default
  • Live streaming restricted or disabled by default
  • Digital gifting and item transfers blocked unless parent-approved
  • Friend requests limited to approved contacts or parent-reviewed accounts
  • Private group joining restricted until reviewed
  • Clear safety logs visible to parents on child-linked accounts

Why defaults matter so much

Most harm does not start with one huge mistake. It starts with small openings:

DMs open
↓
Stranger contact begins
↓
Trust builds
↓
Private communication grows
↓

↓
Risk escalates
Safer defaults close some of those openings before the pattern even starts.

What POSH is calling for

Child accounts must be clearly identified as child accounts

High-risk features should be OFF by default

Parents should get simple, visible control over account risk settings

Platforms should not make parents search through hidden menus just to reduce stranger access

Any feature known to be used in grooming patterns should require stronger child protections

High-risk features that should never be “open by default” for children

  • Open direct messaging
  • Voice chat with strangers
  • Live video interaction
  • Location sharing
  • Anonymous gifting
  • Unrestricted friend requests
  • Account discoverability by unknown adults
If a feature creates direct stranger access, it should never be wide open on a child account by default.

Why this is practical

Platforms already build settings for:

  • privacy
  • purchases
  • screen time
  • content restrictions
  • two-factor security

So there is no real excuse for not building stronger child-account defaults where predictable risks already exist.

This is not about banning technology. It is about designing it more responsibly for minors.

What parents can do right now

Assume the default settings are not safe enough

Check messaging, friends, location, and gifting manually

Set clear family rules around off-platform movement

Use device-level controls and app-level controls together

Talk to your child about why “open by default” does not mean “safe by default”

How this connects to the gifting policy

Gifting is one example of a broader problem:

High-risk features should not be opt-out for children. They should be opt-in, parent-reviewed, and safer by default.

What lawmakers and platforms should hear clearly

Parents should not carry the full burden of child safety while platforms keep risky defaults switched on.

When the risk pattern is known, safer design is no longer optional — it is responsible.

Safer defaults are not overreach. They are overdue.
